Geography of India

Physical Map

Physical Features

India is characterized by a remarkable diversity of physical features. The country can be divided into several physiographic regions:

  • The Himalayan Mountains: The youngest and highest mountain range in the world, forming India's northern boundary. The Himalayas include three parallel ranges - the Greater Himalayas (Himadri), the Lesser Himalayas (Himachal), and the Outer Himalayas (Shiwaliks).
  • The Northern Plains: Formed by the alluvial deposits of the Indus, Ganges, and Brahmaputra rivers. This fertile region is one of the most densely populated areas in the world.
  • The Peninsular Plateau: One of the oldest land masses on earth, consisting of the Central Highlands and the Deccan Plateau.
  • The Coastal Plains: Narrow strips of land between the Western and Eastern Ghats and the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal respectively.
  • The Islands: The Andaman and Nicobar Islands in the Bay of Bengal and the Lakshadweep Islands in the Arabian Sea.

The highest point in India is K2 (Godwin Austen) at 8,611 meters, while the lowest point is Kuttanad in Kerala at 2.2 meters below sea level.

Land Use Challenges

India faces several challenges related to land use:

  • Land Degradation: About 96.4 million hectares (29.3% of total area) is affected by land degradation.
  • Desertification: Approximately 82.64 million hectares is undergoing desertification.
  • Urbanization: Rapid conversion of agricultural land for urban and industrial purposes.
  • Deforestation: Despite increasing forest cover in recent years, historical deforestation has impacted biodiversity.

The government has implemented various land conservation programs including the Integrated Watershed Management Programme and the National Afforestation Programme.

Rainfall and Winds

Monsoon Systems

India's climate is dominated by the monsoon system, which brings seasonal rainfall crucial for agriculture and water resources:

The Southwest Monsoon brings about 75-80% of India's annual rainfall. It originates from the Indian Ocean and Arabian Sea, bringing moisture-laden winds that strike the Western Ghats and the eastern Himalayan ranges.

Onset: Typically begins in Kerala around June 1st and advances northward.

Withdrawal: Begins from northwest India by September and completely withdraws by mid-October.

Also known as the "Retreating Monsoon," it affects primarily the southeastern parts of India, particularly Tamil Nadu, coastal Andhra Pradesh, and parts of Kerala.

Characteristics: Brings significant rainfall to areas that receive less precipitation during the Southwest Monsoon.

Impact: Critical for agriculture in Tamil Nadu and parts of Andhra Pradesh.

These are non-monsoonal precipitation patterns that occur during winter months (December-February).

Origin: Mediterranean Sea and Atlantic Ocean, moving eastward across northern India.

Impact: Bring rainfall to northern plains and snowfall in the Himalayan region. Essential for rabi (winter) crops.

Forests & Wildlife

Forest Types

India has diverse forest ecosystems classified into 16 major types and 251 subtypes. The main categories include:

Tropical Forests

Found in areas with high temperature and rainfall. Includes evergreen, semi-evergreen, moist and dry deciduous forests.

Subtropical Forests

Found in regions with moderate temperature and rainfall, typically at elevations of 1000-2000m.

Temperate Forests

Found in the middle Himalayan ranges, characterized by oak, deodar, pine, and other coniferous trees.

Alpine Forests

Found at high altitudes (3000-4000m), characterized by stunted trees and shrubs.

Mangrove Forests

Found along coastal areas and river deltas, especially in the Sundarbans and Andaman & Nicobar Islands.

Cropping Patterns

India follows different cropping patterns based on climate and soil conditions:

  • Kharif Crops: Grown during monsoon season (June-October). Examples include rice, maize, jowar, bajra, cotton, and groundnut.
  • Rabi Crops: Grown during winter season (October-March). Examples include wheat, barley, gram, mustard, and peas.
  • Zaid Crops: Grown during the short period between Rabi and Kharif seasons. Examples include watermelon, cucumber, and some vegetables.

Food Security Initiatives

India has implemented several programs to ensure food security:

  • Public Distribution System (PDS): Provides subsidized food grains to economically disadvantaged populations.
  • National Food Security Act (2013): Provides legal entitlement to subsidized food grains to approximately two-thirds of India's population.
  • Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS): Provides nutritional support to children and pregnant/lactating mothers.

Economic Impact

Cash crops contribute significantly to India's agricultural exports, which reached $49.6 billion in 2022-23. The cultivation of cash crops has transformed rural economies but also presents challenges:

  • Price Volatility: Cash crops are subject to market fluctuations and international price trends.
  • Input Intensity: Many cash crops require higher inputs of fertilizers, pesticides, and water.
  • Environmental Impact: Intensive cultivation can lead to soil degradation and water pollution.
  • Economic Opportunity: Provides higher income potential compared to traditional food crops.

Agricultural Challenges and Initiatives

Major Challenges
  • Small and Fragmented Landholdings: Average farm size is 1.08 hectares.
  • Water Scarcity: Groundwater depletion and inefficient irrigation practices.
  • Climate Change: Increasing frequency of extreme weather events.
  • Low Productivity: Yields below global averages for many crops.
  • Post-Harvest Losses: Estimated at 15-20% for various crops.
  • Market Access: Limited infrastructure and market linkages for small farmers.
Key Government Initiatives
  • PM-KISAN: Direct income support to farmers (₹6,000 per year).
  • Soil Health Card Scheme: Soil testing and nutrient recommendations.
  • Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana: Crop insurance scheme.
  • e-NAM: Electronic National Agriculture Market for better price discovery.
  • Pradhan Mantri Krishi Sinchayee Yojana: "Per Drop More Crop" initiative for water efficiency.
  • Kisan Credit Card: Provides farmers with affordable credit.
