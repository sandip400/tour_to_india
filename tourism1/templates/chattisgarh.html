Chhattisgarh: The Tribal & Mineral Heart of India

Chhattisgarh is a diverse and vibrant state in central India, known for its rich tribal heritage, dense forests, mineral resources, waterfalls, ancient temples, and traditional arts. It is a key center for mining, steel production, and tribal culture.

  • Location -- Central India
  • Capital -- Raipur
  • Official Language -- Chhattisgarhi, Hindi
  • Area -- 135,192 sq. km
  • Population -- ~29 million (17th most populous state in India)
  • Borders -- Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Jharkhand, Uttar Pradesh
  • Major Rivers -- Mahanadi, Indravati, Sheonath, Hasdo, Rihand
  • Major Festivals -- Bastar Dussehra, Hareli, Pola, Madai, Goncha, Navakhana, Diwali, Teeja
  • State Animal -- Wild Buffalo
  • State Flower -- Rhynchosia (Kessari)
  • State Tree -- Sal Tree
  • State Bird -- Hill Myna

Culture of Chhattisgarh

Chhattisgarh has a rich and diverse culture, shaped by its tribal heritage, history, folk arts, music, festivals, and traditions. Here's an overview of some key aspects of its culture:

1. Language

  • Chhattisgarhi is the main language spoken in the state, along with Hindi which is the official language. Various tribal communities also speak their own dialects like Gondi, Halbi, Bhatri, Sargujia, and Korwa.

2. Literature

  • Chhattisgarh has a rich oral tradition of folk tales, songs, and proverbs that have been passed down through generations.
  • Modern Chhattisgarhi literature includes works by writers like Smt. Vinita Shukla, Dr. Sanjay Alung, and Vidyabhushan Shukla.


3. Art and Craft

  • The state is known for its diverse range of tribal arts and crafts, including Godna art (tattoo art), Dhokra (bell metal craft), Sisal craft, bamboo craft, and wood carving.
  • Traditional paintings like Pithora and Lippan art are unique to the tribal communities of the region.

4. Music

  • Tribal folk music is an integral part of Chhattisgarhi culture, with distinctive styles like Panthi, Raut Nacha, and Pandwani.
  • Traditional musical instruments include mandar, nagara, dhol, bansuri, and dafli.
  • Pandwani, a unique musical narration of tales from the Mahabharata, is famous worldwide, with artists like Teejan Bai gaining international recognition.

5. Dance

  • Chhattisgarh is famous for its vibrant folk dances like Panthi, Raut Nacha, Karma, Saila, and Suwa dance.
  • Each tribal community has its own distinctive dance forms performed during festivals and special occasions.
  • Gaur Maria dance of the Bastar region is particularly famous for its energetic movements and colorful costumes.

6. Cuisine

  • Chhattisgarhi cuisine is simple yet flavorful, with rice being the staple food.
  • Popular dishes include Chila (rice pancake), Farra (steamed rice dumplings), Muthia, and Angakar Roti.
  • Tribal cuisines feature unique ingredients like red ants chutney (Chaprah), bamboo shoots, and forest produce.


7. Theatre and Performing Arts

  • Nacha, a traditional theater form, combines elements of dance, music, and drama.
  • Gammat, Ramleela, and Kaksar are popular theatrical performances in rural areas.

8. Architecture

  • Chhattisgarh's architectural style blends tribal, Hindu, and Jain influences. Notable structures include ancient temples of Sirpur, Bhoramdeo Temple (known as Khajuraho of Chhattisgarh), and Bastar Palace.
  • Traditional tribal houses with mud walls, thatched roofs, and colorful paintings are distinctive features of rural Chhattisgarh.

9. Religious Diversity

  • The state is home to a variety of religions, with Hinduism, tribal faiths, Buddhism, Jainism, and Christianity having significant presence. This diversity is reflected in the state's festivals, rituals, and cultural practices.
  • Tribal communities practice nature worship and ancestor reverence, with sacred groves (Devgudis) being important spiritual sites.

10. Folk Traditions

  • Chhattisgarh is famous for its rich folk traditions, including Panthi, Soowa, Karma, and Gaur Maria dances.
  • Bastar Dussehra, a unique 75-day celebration, is one of the longest festivals in the world.

11. Tribal Philosophy

  • Tribal communities in Chhattisgarh have a deep connection with nature and believe in sustainable living.
  • The concept of community living and sharing resources is central to tribal philosophy.

12. Handicrafts

  • Chhattisgarh is known for its handicrafts such as Dhokra (bell metal craft), bamboo craft, wood carving, terracotta, and iron craft.


Chhattisgarh's culture is deeply rooted in its tribal heritage, and it continues to evolve while maintaining a close connection to nature and traditional values. It's a region where ancient traditions and modern influences coexist beautifully!



Tradition of Chhattisgarh

Chhattisgarh has a rich and diverse tradition deeply rooted in its tribal history, culture, and social practices. Here's an overview of some key traditional aspects of the state:

1. Clothing Tradition

Men

  • Dhoti and Kurta: Traditional Chhattisgarhi men wear dhoti with a kurta, especially during religious ceremonies, weddings, and festivals.
  • Headgear: Colorful turbans or pagris are worn for special occasions.

Women

  • Lugda: Traditional saree worn by tribal women, often in bright colors with distinctive borders.
  • Ornaments: Heavy silver jewelry including neck pieces, anklets, and bangles are worn by tribal women.

2. Wedding Traditions

  • Mama Ghar: The bride stays at her maternal uncle's house before the wedding.
  • Chherta: A ritual where the groom's family visits the bride's home with gifts.
  • Maur Bandhai: The tying of a sacred thread on the groom's forehead.
  • Kohbar: A special room decorated for the newlyweds with tribal art.
  • Mamer: Post-wedding gifts given by the bride's maternal uncle.

3. Music & Dance Traditions

  • Pandwani: A unique form of musical storytelling based on tales from the Mahabharata.
  • Raut Nacha: A traditional dance performed by the Yadav community to celebrate the life of Lord Krishna.
  • Panthi: A devotional dance form of the Satnami community.
  • Karma: A tribal dance performed during the Karma festival to worship the Karam tree.

4. Social & Religious Traditions

  • Ghotul: A traditional tribal institution for youth where they learn social skills, music, and dance.
  • Devgudi: Sacred groves where tribal communities worship their deities.
  • Hareli: Agricultural festival marking the beginning of the farming season, celebrated by worshipping farming tools.

5. Architectural & Handicraft Traditions

  • Dhokra Art: Ancient bell metal craft using the lost-wax technique.
  • Godna Art: Traditional tattoo art that has now evolved into paintings.
  • Bamboo Craft: Intricate items made from bamboo for daily use and decoration.
  • Tribal Paintings: Distinctive styles like Pithora and Lippan that depict tribal life and mythology.

6. Sports & Games

  • Gedi Race: A traditional sport where participants run while balancing on a bamboo stick.
  • Bhaura: A game played with wooden spinning tops.
  • Langdi: A hopping game played by children and adults alike.


Chhattisgarh's traditions beautifully combine tribal heritage, art, food, music, and festivals, making it one of the most culturally authentic states in India.



Festivals of Chhattisgarh

Chhattisgarh is known for its vibrant and diverse festivals, which reflect its rich tribal heritage, agricultural traditions, and religious harmony. Here's a list of some of the major festivals celebrated in the state:

1. Bastar Dussehra (Biggest Tribal Festival)

  • Unlike the traditional 10-day Dussehra, Bastar Dussehra is celebrated for 75 days, making it one of the longest festivals in the world.
  • It's not about Lord Rama's victory but honors the local deity Danteshwari Mai.
  • Features unique rituals like Jatra (chariot procession), Kachan Jatra, and Muria Darbar.
  • Tribal communities from across Bastar participate in colorful processions and rituals.

üìç Time: July - October (Culminates on Vijayadashami)


2. Hareli

  • An agricultural festival marking the beginning of the farming season.
  • Farmers worship their agricultural tools and cattle.
  • Young people plant trees and participate in folk dances.

üìç Time: July - August (Shravan month)


3. Pola Festival

  • Dedicated to bullocks and oxen, who are decorated, worshipped, and given rest from farm work.
  • Bullock races and processions are organized in villages.
  • Special dishes like Chila, Farra, and sweets are prepared.

üìç Time: August - September


4. Navakhana

  • Harvest festival where people offer the first grains of the season to their deities before consuming them.
  • Community feasts are organized with newly harvested crops.
  • Folk dances and music performances are held in villages.

üìç Time: September - October


5. Madai Festival

  • A tribal festival celebrated across Bastar region in honor of various tribal deities.
  • Features colorful processions, animal sacrifices, and tribal dances.
  • Different villages celebrate Madai on different days throughout the winter season.

üìç Time: December - March

6. Goncha Festival

  • Celebrated by the Gond tribe to honor their ancestors and deities.
  • Involves ritual dances, music, and community feasts.

üìç Time: February - March


7. Karma Festival

  • Celebrated by tribal communities to worship the Karam tree, which symbolizes prosperity and good harvest.
  • Young men and women perform the Karma dance around the Karam tree branch.

üìç Time: August - September

8. Teeja

  • A festival for women who pray for the well-being of their husbands.
  • Women wear new clothes, apply mehndi, and sing traditional songs.

üìç Time: August


9. Champaran Mela

  • A fair held at Champaran, the birthplace of Saint Vallabhacharya, founder of the Vallabh sect.
  • Devotees from across India gather to participate in religious discourses and rituals.

üìç Time: February - March


10. Rajim Kumbh Mela

  • A month-long religious fair held at the confluence of three rivers - Mahanadi, Pairi, and Sondur.
  • Thousands of devotees take a holy dip and participate in religious ceremonies.
  • Cultural programs, handicraft exhibitions, and folk performances are organized.

üìç Time: February (Magha month)


11. Bhoramdeo Festival

  • Celebrated at the Bhoramdeo Temple, known as the "Khajuraho of Chhattisgarh."
  • Features cultural performances, crafts fair, and religious ceremonies.

üìç Time: February - March

12. Mainpat Mahotsav

  • A winter festival celebrated at Mainpat, known as the "Mini Shimla" of Chhattisgarh.
  • Showcases the culture and traditions of the Tibetan community settled there.

üìç Time: December - January


13. Lokotsav

  • A state-sponsored folk festival that showcases the rich cultural heritage of Chhattisgarh.
  • Features folk dances, music, handicrafts, and tribal art exhibitions.

üìç Time: January

14. National Tribal Dance Festival

  • A major event where tribal dance troupes from across India and abroad participate.
  • Celebrates the rich tribal heritage and promotes cultural exchange.

üìç Time: December

Foods of Chhattisgarh

Chhattisgarh has a rich and diverse culinary tradition that blends tribal flavors with regional influences. Chhattisgarhi cuisine is known for its simplicity, use of local ingredients, and distinctive preparation methods. Here's a look at some of the most iconic foods of Chhattisgarh:

1. Staple Food of Chhattisgarh

  • Rice: The main staple food, with over 20,000 varieties of rice traditionally grown in the state.
  • Basi: Leftover rice soaked in water overnight, eaten for breakfast with salt, onion, and chili.

2. Chhattisgarhi Breads

  • Chila: Savory pancakes made from rice flour or gram flour.
  • Angakar Roti: Thick bread made from rice flour, cooked on hot coals.
  • Paan Roti: Rice flour bread cooked on sal leaves, giving it a unique flavor.

3. Famous Chhattisgarhi Dishes

Vegetarian Dishes

  • Farra: Steamed rice dumplings filled with spiced gram flour, often served with chutney.
  • Muthia: Steamed dumplings made from rice flour and vegetables.
  • Dubki Kadhi: A yogurt-based curry with gram flour dumplings.
  • Thethari: A spicy dish made from rice flour and buttermilk.
  • Aamat: A sweet and sour curry made with vegetables and mango.

Non-Vegetarian Dishes

  • Mahua Chicken: Chicken cooked with mahua flowers, giving it a unique sweet flavor.
  • Chaprah: A chutney made from red ants, considered a delicacy in tribal areas.
  • Bafauri: Steamed gram flour dumplings with meat.
  • Jhurka: A spicy meat curry popular in rural areas.

Tribal Specialties

  • Bamboo Shoot Curry: A delicacy made from tender bamboo shoots.
  • Lal Bhaji: A dish made from red amaranth leaves.
  • Bohar Bhaji: Wild mushrooms cooked with minimal spices.

Dal & Accompaniments

  • Bori: Sun-dried lentil dumplings used in curries and soups.
  • Dehati Dal: A simple preparation of local lentils with minimal spices.

4. Chhattisgarhi Sweets

  • Khurmi: Deep-fried sweet made from wheat flour and jaggery.
  • Bidiya: Sweet dumplings made from rice flour and filled with chana dal and jaggery.
  • Kusli: Deep-fried sweet spirals made from rice flour.
  • Gulgula: Sweet fritters made from wheat flour and jaggery.
  • Tikhur Barfi: A sweet made from tikhur (a medicinal root) starch.

5. Street Food of Chhattisgarh

  • Chila: Rice or gram flour pancakes served with chutney.
  • Bara: Spicy lentil fritters, a popular snack.
  • Bhajia: Vegetable fritters made with gram flour batter.
  • Sabudana Khichdi: A savory dish made from tapioca pearls.
  • Poha: Flattened rice cooked with spices and peanuts.

6. Famous Drinks

  • Mahua: A traditional alcoholic beverage made from mahua flowers.
  • Sulfi: A local drink extracted from the sap of the sulfi palm.
  • Kosna: A refreshing drink made from rice water.

7. Special Festival Foods

  • Chousela: A special dish made during Hareli festival, consisting of rice, curd, and jaggery.
  • Dehati Bada: Spicy lentil fritters prepared during festivals.
  • Arsa: Sweet deep-fried discs made from rice flour and jaggery, prepared during celebrations.

History of Chhattisgarh

Chhattisgarh has a rich and diverse history that spans ancient civilizations, medieval kingdoms, British colonial rule, and the modern era. The region has played a significant role in India's cultural, political, and economic history.

1. Ancient Chhattisgarh (Before 4th Century CE)

  • Prehistoric Period: Rock paintings in Singhanpur and Kabra Pahad indicate human settlement dating back to 10,000 BCE.
  • Dakshina Kosala: The region was known as "Dakshina Kosala" in ancient texts like the Ramayana and Mahabharata.
  • Maurya & Gupta Influence: The region was part of the Maurya Empire and later the Gupta Empire, with Buddhist influences evident in sites like Sirpur.

2. Medieval Chhattisgarh (4th - 18th Century)

Sarabhpurias & Nala Dynasty (4th - 7th Century):

  • The Sarabhpurias ruled from Sirpur, which became a major center of Buddhism and Hinduism.
  • Archaeological excavations at Sirpur have revealed temples, monasteries, and a unique blend of religious architecture.

Kalchuri Dynasty (10th - 12th Century):

  • The Kalchuris ruled from Ratanpur and Raipur, building numerous temples and water bodies.
  • The famous Bhoramdeo Temple (known as "Khajuraho of Chhattisgarh") was built during this period.

Haihaya Dynasty (14th - 18th Century):

  • The Haihayas ruled from Ratanpur and divided their kingdom into 18 garhs (forts), giving the region its name "Chhattisgarh" (36 forts).
  • This period saw the development of unique cultural traditions and architectural styles.

Maratha Rule (18th Century):

  • The Marathas conquered the region in 1741 and ruled until the British took control.
  • They introduced administrative reforms and built several forts and temples.

3. British Colonial Period (1818 - 1947)

  • The British East India Company took control of the region in 1818.
  • Chhattisgarh was made part of the Central Provinces and Berar in 1861.
  • The region saw several tribal revolts against British rule, including the Halba Rebellion (1774-79) and the Bhumkal Rebellion (1910).

4. Tribal Revolts & Freedom Struggle

Major Tribal Uprisings

  • Halba Rebellion (1774-79): Led by Halba tribals against the Marathas and later the British.
  • Bhumkal Rebellion (1910): A major uprising in Bastar led by Gunda Dhur against British exploitation.
  • Jungle Satyagraha (1930s): Tribal protests against forest laws that restricted their traditional rights.

Freedom Movement

  • The region actively participated in the Indian freedom movement with leaders like Thakur Pyarelal Singh and Minimata.
  • The Jungle Satyagraha merged with the broader Civil Disobedience Movement led by Mahatma Gandhi.

5. Post-Independence Period (1947 - 2000)

  • After independence, Chhattisgarh became part of Madhya Pradesh.
  • The region saw industrial development with the establishment of Bhilai Steel Plant in 1955.
  • Demand for a separate state grew stronger in the 1970s and 1980s.
  • The Chhattisgarh Rajya Nirman Manch led by Chandulal Chandrakar spearheaded the movement for statehood.

6. Formation of Chhattisgarh State (2000)

  • Chhattisgarh was officially formed on November 1, 2000, as the 26th state of India.
  • Raipur was designated as the capital city.
  • Ajit Jogi became the first Chief Minister of the new state.
  • The state was created to address the distinct cultural identity and developmental needs of the region.

7. Modern Chhattisgarh (2000 - Present)

Development & Challenges

  • The state has focused on industrial development, particularly in mining, steel, and power sectors.
  • Chhattisgarh has emerged as a power hub and is known as the "Rice Bowl of India."
  • The state has faced challenges related to Naxalism, tribal welfare, and balanced development.

Recent Developments

  • Focus on eco-tourism, tribal welfare, and sustainable development.
  • Initiatives to preserve tribal culture and heritage.
  • Development of Naya Raipur (now Atal Nagar) as a planned city and new administrative capital.

8. Archaeological Heritage

  • Sirpur: A major archaeological site with Buddhist and Hindu monuments dating back to the 5th-8th centuries.
  • Malhar: Ancient temple complex with inscriptions from the Kalchuri period.
  • Bhoramdeo Temple: 11th-century temple known for its intricate erotic sculptures.
  • Rock Paintings: Prehistoric rock art at Singhanpur, Kabra Pahad, and Gotitola.


Chhattisgarh has witnessed ancient civilizations, tribal kingdoms, colonial rule, freedom struggles, and modern development. It remains a center of tribal culture, mineral resources, and industrial growth in India. üö©üìú

Tourist Spots of Chhattisgarh üèñÔ∏èüèîÔ∏è

Chhattisgarh is a diverse state with waterfalls, forests, tribal areas, heritage sites, and cultural hubs. Here's a categorized list of the best tourist places in Chhattisgarh:



1. Waterfalls & Natural Wonders üèûÔ∏è

  • Chitrakote Falls - India's widest waterfall (Niagara of India), horseshoe-shaped falls on Indravati River
  • Tirathgarh Falls - Multi-tiered waterfall with natural swimming pools
  • Amrit Dhara Waterfall - Scenic falls in Koriya district
  • Kanger Valley National Park - Limestone caves, waterfalls, and pristine forests
  • Mainpat - Known as "Mini Shimla," a plateau with scenic beauty and Tibetan settlements

2. Heritage & Archaeological Sites üèõÔ∏è

  • Sirpur - Ancient archaeological site with Buddhist and Hindu monuments dating back to 5th-8th centuries.
  • Bhoramdeo Temple - 11th-century temple known as "Khajuraho of Chhattisgarh" for its erotic sculptures.
  • Bastar Palace - Historical palace in Jagdalpur showcasing royal heritage.
  • Malhar - Ancient temple complex with inscriptions from the Kalchuri period.
  • Ratanpur Fort - Historical fort with ancient temples and a sacred lake.

3. Wildlife & Nature Destinations üå≥üêØ

  • Achanakmar Tiger Reserve - Dense forests, tigers, leopards, and diverse flora.
  • Barnawapara Wildlife Sanctuary - Home to wild buffalo, leopards, and over 150 bird species.
  • Kanger Valley National Park - Biodiversity hotspot with unique caves and waterfalls.
  • Sitanadi Wildlife Sanctuary - Rich biodiversity along the Sitanadi River.
  • Gomarda Wildlife Sanctuary - Known for sloth bears and diverse bird species.

4. Tribal Culture & Craft Centers üè∫

  • Bastar Tribal Museum - Showcases the rich tribal heritage of the region.
  • Purkhauti Muktangan - Open-air museum in Raipur displaying tribal art and culture.
  • Kondagaon Art & Craft Center - Famous for Dhokra (bell metal) art and iron craft.
  • Narayanpur Handicraft Market - Traditional tribal crafts and artifacts.
  • Tribal villages of Abujhmad - One of the most pristine tribal regions in India.

5. Religious Destinations ‚õ©Ô∏è

  • Danteshwari Temple (Dantewada) - One of the 52 Shakti Peethas, dedicated to Goddess Danteshwari.
  • Dongargarh (Rajrajeshwari Temple) - Hill temple accessed by 1,000 steps or ropeway.
  • Champaran - Birthplace of Saint Vallabhacharya, with a significant temple complex.
  • Rajim - Temple town at the confluence of three rivers, known as "Prayag of Chhattisgarh."
  • Mahamaya Temple (Ratanpur) - Ancient temple dedicated to Goddess Mahamaya.

6. Adventure & Offbeat Destinations üé¢üöµ

  • Chaiturgarh - Hill station with trekking opportunities and panoramic views.
  • Kutumsar Cave - One of India's longest natural caves with unique blind fish species.
  • Kailash Gufa - Limestone cave with natural Shiva lingam formations.
  • Girodhpuri - Birthplace of Guru Ghasidas, founder of the Satnami sect.
  • Jashpur - Known as "Nagpur of Chhattisgarh" for its orange cultivation and waterfalls.

7. Urban Attractions üèôÔ∏è

  • Raipur - State capital with Purkhauti Muktangan, Mahant Ghasidas Memorial Museum, and Nandan Van Zoo.
  • Naya Raipur (Atal Nagar) - Planned city with Jungle Safari, Central Park, and modern architecture.
  • Bilaspur - Commercial center with Arpa River, Achanakmar Tiger Reserve, and Ratanpur Fort nearby.
  • Bhilai - Industrial city with Maitri Garden, Bhilai Steel Plant, and Japanese Garden.
  • Durg - Twin city of Bhilai with historical sites and cultural attractions.

üåæ Agriculture & Major Crops in Chhattisgarh

Chhattisgarh is known as the "Rice Bowl of India," with agriculture being the primary occupation for about 80% of the rural population.

üîπ Key Agricultural Features

  • One of India's largest producers of Rice, with over 20,000 varieties traditionally grown.
  • Fertile Land: Thanks to the Mahanadi River basin and its tributaries.
  • Climate: Supports both Kharif & Rabi crops.
  • Irrigation: Mahanadi, Indravati, and Sheonath rivers support farming.
