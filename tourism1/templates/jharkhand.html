  • Major Festivals -- Sarhul, Karma, Tusu, Sohrai, Diwali, Holi, Durga Puja
  • State Animal -- Elephant
  • State Flower -- Palash (Flame of the Forest)

Culture of Jharkhand

Jharkhand has a rich and diverse culture, shaped by its tribal heritage, history, art, music, festivals, and traditions. Here's an overview of some key aspects of its culture:

1. Language

  • Hindi is the official language of Jharkhand, but the state is home to numerous tribal languages including Santhali, Mundari, Ho, Kurukh (Oraon), and Kharia. Santhali has been included in the 8th Schedule of the Indian Constitution and has its own script called Ol Chiki.

2. Tribal Heritage

  • Jharkhand is home to 32 tribal groups including Santhal, Munda, Oraon, Ho, Kharia, Gond, and Birhor, each with their distinct cultural practices, languages, and traditions.
  • Tribal communities maintain their unique identity through their customs, festivals, dances, and social structures.


3. Art and Craft

  • The state is known for its diverse range of tribal art forms, including Sohrai and Khovar paintings, which are traditional wall art created during harvest and wedding seasons.
  • Bamboo and cane crafts, wood carving, metal work, and tribal jewelry are significant crafts of the region.

4. Music

  • Tribal folk music is an integral part of Jharkhand's culture, with instruments like mandar, nagara, dhol, flute, and jhanj.
  • Jhumar, Domkach, and Mardana Jhumar are popular folk music forms.
  • Panch Parganiya songs are popular in the Chotanagpur region.
  • Ceremonial songs for birth, marriage, and festivals are an important part of tribal life.

5. Dance

  • Jharkhand is famous for its vibrant tribal dances like Chhau, Santhal, Munda, Oraon, and Ho dances.
  • Karma, Jhumar, Jadur, Sohrai, and Sarhul are major dance forms performed during festivals.
  • Chhau dance, a semi-classical dance form, is particularly famous in the Seraikela region.

6. Cuisine

  • Jharkhand cuisine is simple, nutritious, and closely connected to nature, with rice as the staple food.
  • Dhuska (deep-fried rice and gram flour bread), Pittha (rice flour dumplings), and Handia (rice beer) are local specialties.
  • Rugra (mushroom), bamboo shoots, and various forest produce form an important part of the tribal diet.


7. Theatre and Cinema

  • Jatra, a traditional form of theater, is popular in rural areas.
  • Jharkhand has a growing film industry with movies made in local languages like Nagpuri, Santhali, and Khortha.

8. Architecture

  • Traditional tribal houses (mud houses with thatched roofs) reflect the architectural style of the region.
  • Ancient temples, rock-cut caves, and colonial buildings add to the architectural diversity.

9. Religious Diversity

  • The state is home to a variety of religions, with Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, and tribal religions (Sarna) having a significant presence. This diversity is reflected in the state's festivals, rituals, and cultural practices.

10. Folk Traditions

  • Jharkhand is rich in folk traditions, with stories, songs, and dances passed down through generations.
  • Tribal communities have their own creation myths, legends, and folklore that reflect their worldview and relationship with nature.

11. Tribal Governance

  • Many tribal communities maintain traditional governance systems like Manjhi Parishad (Santhal) and Parha (Munda), which handle community affairs and disputes.

12. Handicrafts

  • Jharkhand is known for its handicrafts such as bamboo work, dokra (metal casting), terracotta, and tribal jewelry.


Jharkhand's culture is deeply rooted in its tribal heritage and connection to nature, and it continues to evolve while maintaining a close connection to its traditions. It's a region where modernity and tradition coexist beautifully!



Tradition of Jharkhand

Jharkhand has a rich and diverse tradition deeply rooted in its tribal heritage, culture, and social practices. Here's an overview of some key traditional aspects of the state:

1. Clothing Tradition

Men

  • Traditional Attire: Men typically wear dhoti or lungi with a kurta or shirt. Tribal men often wear a gamcha (towel) on their shoulders.
  • Headgear: Different tribes have their own traditional headgear, often adorned with feathers or flowers during festivals.

Women

  • Saree: Tribal women wear colorful sarees in distinctive styles. Santhal women wear shorter sarees with red borders called Panchi.
  • Jewelry: Heavy silver jewelry, including necklaces, anklets, and bangles, is an important part of tribal attire.

2. Wedding Traditions

  • Tribal Weddings: Each tribe has its own unique wedding customs. For example, Santhal marriages involve rituals like Bapla Bonga (worship) and Nim Da (ceremonial bath).
  • Bride Price: In many tribal communities, the groom's family pays a bride price (gonong) to the bride's family, unlike the dowry system.
  • Community Involvement: Weddings are community affairs with the entire village participating in the celebrations.
  • Traditional Music and Dance: Weddings feature traditional music and dance performances specific to each tribe.

3. Music & Dance Traditions

  • Tribal Dances: Each tribe has its own distinctive dance forms, such as Santhal's Sohrai and Baha, Oraon's Karma and Jhumair, and Munda's Jadur.
  • Chhau Dance: A semi-classical martial dance form from Seraikela, performed with elaborate masks.
  • Traditional Instruments: Mandar, nagara, dhol, bansuri (flute), and jhanj are commonly used in tribal music.
  • Ceremonial Songs: Special songs for birth, harvest, marriage, and death ceremonies.

4. Social & Religious Traditions

  • Sarna Religion: Many tribes follow Sarna, a nature-worshipping religion that reveres the sacred grove (Sarna Sthal).
  • Tribal Governance: Traditional systems like Manjhi Parishad (Santhal) and Parha (Munda) handle community affairs and disputes.
  • Harvest Festivals: Sarhul, Karma, and Sohrai are major harvest festivals celebrated with rituals, music, and dance.

5. Architectural & Handicraft Traditions

  • Traditional Houses: Mud houses with thatched roofs, often decorated with Khovar and Sohrai paintings during festivals and weddings.
  • Tribal Art: Distinctive painting styles like Sohrai (harvest festival art) and Khovar (wedding chamber art).
  • Bamboo & Cane Craft: Baskets, mats, and household items made from bamboo and cane.
  • Dokra Art: Ancient metal casting technique using the lost-wax method to create figurines and jewelry.

6. Sports & Games

  • Traditional Games: Tribal communities play games like Sekkor (a form of hockey), Kati (stick game), and Bahu-Chor (bride stealing game).
  • Archery: A traditional skill and sport, especially among the Santhal and Ho tribes.
  • Football: Extremely popular across the state, with many tribal youth excelling in the sport.


Jharkhand's traditions beautifully combine tribal heritage, art, food, music, and festivals, making it one of the most culturally vibrant states in India with a unique identity rooted in its indigenous communities.



Festivals of Jharkhand

Jharkhand is known for its vibrant and diverse festivals, which reflect its rich tribal heritage, cultural traditions, and religious harmony. Here's a list of some of the major festivals celebrated in the state:

1. Sarhul (Spring Festival)

  • One of the most important tribal festivals, celebrating the beginning of spring and the flowering of the Sal tree.
  • Tribal communities worship the Sal tree, which is considered sacred, and pray for a good harvest.
  • Colorful processions, traditional music, and dance performances mark the celebrations.
  • The festival symbolizes the marriage of Earth and Sky, representing fertility and renewal.

üìç Time: March - April


2. Karma Festival

  • A major harvest festival celebrated by the Oraon, Munda, and Ho tribes.
  • The Karma tree is worshipped as a symbol of prosperity and good fortune.
  • Young men and women perform the Karma dance around the sacred tree.
  • The festival includes feasting, singing, and community celebrations.

üìç Time: August - September


3. Sohrai (Harvest Festival)

  • The biggest harvest festival of Jharkhand, celebrated after the rice harvest.
  • Homes are decorated with traditional Sohrai paintings depicting animals and nature.
  • Cattle are worshipped, decorated, and taken out in processions.
  • The festival includes traditional music, dance, and feasting.

üìç Time: October - November


4. Tusu Festival

  • Celebrated primarily by the Kurmi community during the harvest season.
  • Young girls make Tusu dolls and decorate them elaborately.
  • The festival culminates with the immersion of the Tusu dolls in rivers or ponds.
  • Traditional Tusu songs and dances are performed during the celebrations.

üìç Time: January (during Makar Sankranti)


5. Karam Festival

  • A major festival of the Oraon, Baiga, and Munda tribes.
  • The Karam tree branch is brought to the village and planted in the sacred ground.
  • Young men and women perform the Karam dance around the tree.
  • The festival is celebrated to ensure good crops, prosperity, and well-being.

üìç Time: August - September

6. Baha Festival

  • A spring festival celebrated by the Santhal, Munda, and Ho tribes.
  • The festival marks the blooming of flowers and the beginning of spring.
  • Traditional rituals, music, and dance performances are part of the celebrations.

üìç Time: February - March


7. Jitia Puja

  • A festival observed by mothers for the well-being and long life of their children.
  • Women observe a strict fast without water for 24 hours.
  • The festival includes rituals, storytelling, and prayers.

üìç Time: September

8. Diwali & Chhath Puja

  • Widely celebrated across Jharkhand with traditional fervor.
  • Homes are decorated with lights, and fireworks are displayed.
  • Chhath Puja, dedicated to the Sun God, is observed with great devotion, especially in urban areas.

üìç Time: October - November


9. Mage Parab

  • A harvest festival celebrated by the Ho tribe.
  • The festival marks the end of the harvest season and the beginning of the new year.
  • Traditional rituals, music, dance, and feasting are part of the celebrations.

üìç Time: January - February


10. Eid & Muharram

  • Celebrated by the Muslim community across Jharkhand.
  • Eid celebrations include special prayers, feasting, and community gatherings.
  • Muharram processions are taken out in major cities and towns.

üìç Time: Based on Islamic Calendar


11. Durga Puja & Dussehra

  • Celebrated with great enthusiasm, especially in urban areas.
  • Elaborate pandals are set up, and cultural programs are organized.
  • The festival culminates with the immersion of Durga idols in rivers and lakes.

üìç Time: September - October

12. Christmas

  • Celebrated by the Christian community, which has a significant presence in Jharkhand.
  • Churches are decorated, and special midnight masses are held.
  • Ranchi, the capital city, is known for its Christmas celebrations.

üìç Time: December 25


13. Rohini Festival

  • A traditional festival celebrated by the Asur tribe.
  • The festival marks the beginning of the agricultural season.
  • Traditional rituals, music, and dance performances are part of the celebrations.

üìç Time: May - June

14. Shravani Mela

  • A month-long fair held at Baidyanath Dham in Deoghar, one of the twelve Jyotirlingas.
  • Devotees carry holy water from the Ganges and walk barefoot to offer it to Lord Shiva.
  • The fair attracts millions of pilgrims from across India.

üìç Time: July - August

Foods of Jharkhand

Jharkhand has a rich and diverse culinary tradition that is simple, nutritious, and closely connected to nature. The cuisine is influenced by tribal traditions and uses locally available ingredients, including forest produce. Here's a look at some of the most iconic foods of Jharkhand:

1. Staple Food of Jharkhand

  • Rice: The most important staple food, consumed with various side dishes.
  • Madua Roti: Flatbread made from finger millet (ragi), a nutritious alternative to wheat.

2. Traditional Breads

  • Dhuska: Deep-fried bread made from rice and gram flour, a popular breakfast item.
  • Pittha: Steamed rice flour dumplings with sweet or savory fillings.
  • Chilka Roti: A thin pancake made from rice flour, often served with vegetables or meat.

3. Famous Jharkhand Dishes

Vegetarian Dishes

  • Rugra: A wild mushroom curry, considered a delicacy in tribal cuisine.
  • Bamboo Shoot Curry: Young bamboo shoots cooked with spices and herbs.
  • Koinar Saag: A nutritious leafy vegetable dish, often cooked with garlic and chili.
  • Sanai Phool Bhaji: A curry made from jute flowers, rich in iron and other nutrients.

Non-Vegetarian Dishes

  • Desi Chicken: Country chicken cooked with minimal spices, a popular tribal dish.
  • Handia Chicken: Chicken cooked with rice beer (handia), giving it a unique flavor.
  • Pitha Meat: A combination of rice dumplings and meat curry.
  • Sutli Kebab: Minced meat kebabs tied with cotton thread while cooking.

Tribal Specialties

  • Handia: Traditional rice beer, an integral part of tribal culture and ceremonies.
  • Mahua: An alcoholic drink made from the flowers of the mahua tree.
  • Putoo: Steamed rice flour cakes, often served with meat or vegetables.

Dal & Accompaniments

  • Ghugni: A spicy curry made from yellow peas, often served with rice or bread.
  • Jharkhand Dal: A mix of various lentils cooked with minimal spices.

4. Sweets & Desserts

  • Arsa: Deep-fried sweet made from rice flour and jaggery.
  • Thekua: A traditional sweet made during festivals, especially Chhath Puja.
  • Malpua: Sweet pancakes soaked in sugar syrup.
  • Khaja: Layered pastry deep-fried and soaked in sugar syrup.
  • Lai: A sweet made from puffed rice and jaggery.

5. Street Food of Jharkhand

  • Litti Chokha: Baked wheat flour balls filled with sattu (roasted gram flour) served with mashed potatoes and brinjal.
  • Dhuska with Ghugni: A popular street food combination, especially for breakfast.
  • Chaat: Various types of spicy snacks, including golgappa (pani puri) and aloo tikki.
  • Jhalmuri: Puffed rice mixed with spices, peanuts, and vegetables.
  • Bhunja: Roasted grains and nuts, a popular snack during festivals.

6. Beverages

  • Handia: Traditional rice beer, consumed during festivals and ceremonies.
  • Mahua: An alcoholic drink made from mahua flowers, popular in tribal areas.
  • Sattu Sharbat: A cooling drink made from roasted gram flour, especially popular in summer.

7. Special Festival Foods

  • Dhuska and Ghugni: Prepared during festivals like Sarhul and Karma.
  • Arsa and Thekua: Traditional sweets made during festivals.
  • Handia: Rice beer prepared for special occasions and festivals.

History of Jharkhand

Jharkhand has a rich and diverse history that spans ancient tribal civilizations, medieval kingdoms, British colonial rule, and the modern struggle for statehood. The region has played a significant role in India's freedom movement and tribal rights activism.

1. Ancient Jharkhand (Before 6th Century CE)

  • Prehistoric Era: Rock paintings in Hazaribagh and other areas suggest human habitation dating back to the Mesolithic period.
  • Tribal Settlements: The region was inhabited by various tribal groups like the Mundas, Santhals, Oraons, and Hos, who had their own governance systems.
  • Magadha Empire: Parts of Jharkhand were under the influence of the ancient Magadha Empire during the time of Ashoka.

2. Medieval Period (6th - 16th Century)

Nagvanshi Dynasty (6th - 12th Century):

  • One of the oldest ruling dynasties in Jharkhand, established their capital at Chhotanagpur.
  • Built temples and promoted agriculture in the region.
  • The dynasty continued to rule parts of Jharkhand until the 19th century.

Other Regional Kingdoms:

  • Pala Empire controlled parts of northern Jharkhand.
  • Chero dynasty ruled the Palamu region.
  • Various tribal chieftains maintained semi-autonomous status in different parts of the region.

Muslim Influence (13th - 16th Century):

  • The Delhi Sultanate and later the Bengal Sultanate had limited control over parts of Jharkhand.
  • The region's difficult terrain and tribal resistance limited outside control.
  • Local chieftains often maintained autonomy by paying tribute to larger powers.

Mughal Period (16th - 18th Century):

  • Emperor Akbar's general Man Singh brought parts of Jharkhand under Mughal control.
  • The Mughals established military outposts but had limited administrative control.
  • Local rulers continued to govern under Mughal suzerainty.

3. British Colonial Period (1765 - 1947)

  • After the Battle of Buxar (1764), the East India Company gained control over Bengal, Bihar, and parts of Jharkhand.
  • The Permanent Settlement Act of 1793 introduced new land revenue systems that disrupted traditional tribal land ownership.
  • Christian missionaries established schools and hospitals in the region, leading to conversions among tribal communities.

4. Tribal Revolts & Freedom Struggle (1765 - 1947)

Major Tribal Uprisings

  • Santhal Rebellion (1855-56): Led by Sido and Kanhu Murmu against British exploitation and the zamindari system.
  • Kol Rebellion (1831-32): One of the earliest tribal revolts against British land policies.
  • Munda Rebellion (1899-1900): Led by Birsa Munda, known as "Dharti Aba" (Father of the Earth), against British oppression and exploitation.

Impact of Industrialization

  • Discovery of coal and minerals led to rapid industrialization and mining activities.
  • Tribal lands were acquired for mining and industries, leading to displacement and unrest.

Freedom Movement in Jharkhand

  • Tana Bhagat Movement: A tribal religious movement that turned into a freedom struggle against the British.
  • Quit India Movement (1942): Strong participation from the region, with notable leaders like Jaipal Singh Munda.
  • Adivasi Mahasabha: Formed in 1938 to advocate for tribal rights and autonomy.
  • Jharkhand Movement (1930s onwards):
    • The demand for a separate state began in the 1930s, led by the Adivasi Mahasabha.
    • Jaipal Singh Munda, an Oxford-educated tribal leader and Olympian, became the face of the movement.
    • The Jharkhand Party was formed in 1950 to advocate for a separate tribal state.

5. Post-Independence Period (1947 - 2000)

Part of Bihar (1947-2000)

  • After independence, the Jharkhand region became part of Bihar state.
  • The struggle for a separate state continued with various organizations leading the movement.
  • The All Jharkhand Students Union (AJSU) and Jharkhand Mukti Morcha (JMM) emerged as key political forces.

Formation of Jharkhand State

  • The Bihar Reorganization Act was passed in 2000, leading to the creation of Jharkhand as India's 28th state on November 15, 2000.
  • Ranchi became the capital of the new state.
  • The first government was formed under Chief Minister Babulal Marandi of the BJP.

6. Present-Day Jharkhand (2000 - Present)

  • Economic Development: Focus on mining, industry, and infrastructure development.
  • Challenges: Naxalite insurgency, poverty, tribal displacement, and environmental concerns.
  • Cultural Renaissance: Efforts to preserve and promote tribal languages, art, and culture.
  • Tourism Growth: Development of eco-tourism, religious tourism, and adventure tourism.


Jharkhand's history is a story of tribal resilience, resistance against exploitation, and the struggle for identity and autonomy. From ancient tribal settlements to becoming a modern state, Jharkhand continues to balance its rich cultural heritage with industrial development and progress. üö©üìú
