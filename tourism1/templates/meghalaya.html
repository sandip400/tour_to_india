Meghalaya: The Abode of Clouds

Meghalaya is a picturesque state in northeastern India, known for its lush green landscapes, waterfalls, living root bridges, caves, and rich tribal culture. It is one of the wettest places on Earth and home to diverse flora and fauna.

  • Location -- Northeastern India
  • Capital -- Shillong
  • Official Languages -- English, Khasi, Garo, Jaintia
  • Area -- 22,429 sq. km
  • Population -- ~3.3 million
  • Borders -- Assam (north and east), Bangladesh (south and west)
  • Major Rivers -- Umiam, Kynshi, Myntdu, Ganol
  • Major Festivals -- Shad Suk Mynsiem, Behdienkhlam, Wangala, Nongkrem Dance, Christmas
  • State Animal -- Clouded Leopard
  • State Flower -- Lady's Slipper Orchid
  • State Tree -- White Teak (Gmelina arborea)
  • State Bird -- Hill Myna

Culture of Meghalaya

Meghalaya has a rich and diverse culture, shaped by its three main tribes - Khasi, Garo, and Jaintia. Each tribe has its own unique traditions, language, customs, and festivals. Here's an overview of some key aspects of Meghalayan culture:

1. Language

  • Meghalaya has three main languages corresponding to its three major tribes: Khasi, Garo, and Jaintia (Pnar). English is the official language of the state and is widely spoken, especially in urban areas. These languages belong to different language families - Khasi and Jaintia are Austro-Asiatic languages, while Garo belongs to the Tibeto-Burman family.

2. Social Structure

  • Meghalaya is one of the few matrilineal societies in the world. In the Khasi, Jaintia, and Garo tribes, lineage and inheritance pass through the female line. The youngest daughter (Khadduh) inherits the family property, and children take their mother's surname.
  • The maternal uncle (Kni) plays an important role in family decisions and the upbringing of children.


3. Art and Craft

  • Meghalaya is known for its bamboo and cane crafts, with items like baskets, mats, and furniture being common.
  • Weaving is an important craft, with each tribe having distinctive patterns and designs. The Garo tribe is known for its colorful Dakmanda (traditional dress), while the Khasi and Jaintia tribes create beautiful Jainsem and Dhara garments.

4. Music

  • Music is an integral part of Meghalayan culture, with each tribe having its own traditional instruments and songs.
  • The Khasi are known for their folk songs called "Phawar" and instruments like the Duitara (a stringed instrument) and Ksing (drums).
  • The Garos have a rich tradition of folk songs and use instruments like the Nagra (drums) and Chigring (a stringed instrument).
  • Shillong, the capital, is often called the "Rock Capital of India" due to its vibrant Western music scene.

5. Dance

  • Traditional dances are performed during festivals and special occasions.
  • The Khasis perform the Ka Shad Suk Mynsiem, a thanksgiving dance to celebrate a good harvest.
  • The Garos have the Wangala dance, also known as the "Hundred Drums Festival."
  • The Jaintias perform the Behdienkhlam dance during their festival of the same name.

6. Cuisine

  • Meghalayan cuisine is simple but flavorful, with rice being the staple food.
  • Jadoh (rice cooked with pork or chicken) is a popular Khasi dish.
  • Tungrymbai (fermented soybean dish) is a delicacy among the Khasis.
  • The Garos enjoy Nakham Bitchi (dried fish with chili) and Wak Pura (pork with bamboo shoots).
  • Locally brewed rice beer called Kyat is popular across all tribes.


7. Architecture

  • Traditional Khasi houses are made of wood, bamboo, and thatch.
  • The living root bridges, created by training the roots of rubber trees across streams, are a unique architectural marvel of the Khasi and Jaintia Hills.
  • Modern Shillong has a mix of colonial and contemporary architecture.

8. Religious Beliefs

  • Christianity is the predominant religion in Meghalaya, with over 70% of the population being Christian.
  • Traditional indigenous religions are still practiced, especially in rural areas.
  • The Khasis traditionally believe in "Ka Niam Khasi," which centers around the worship of God (U Blei) and reverence for ancestors.
  • Sacred groves (Law Kyntang) are protected forest areas considered sacred by the Khasis.

9. Festivals

  • Festivals in Meghalaya are often related to agriculture, religion, or seasonal changes.
  • Major festivals include Shad Suk Mynsiem (Khasi spring dance festival), Behdienkhlam (Jaintia festival to ward off plague and disease), and Wangala (Garo harvest festival).
  • Christmas is widely celebrated across the state due to the large Christian population.

10. Traditional Attire

  • Khasi women wear the Jainsem or Dhara (a two-piece garment) with a blouse underneath.
  • Khasi men wear the Jymphong (a long sleeveless coat) and a turban called the Jain Kyrshah.
  • Garo women wear the Dakmanda, a colorful woven cloth wrapped around the body.
  • Garo men wear the Gando, a loincloth, and the Kotip, a turban.

11. Arts and Literature

  • Oral traditions, including folktales and legends, are an important part of Meghalayan culture.
  • Modern Khasi literature began with the introduction of the Roman script by Christian missionaries in the 19th century.
  • Prominent Khasi writers include Soso Tham and Helen Giri.


Meghalaya's culture is a beautiful blend of traditional tribal practices and modern influences. The state's unique matrilineal system, combined with its rich artistic traditions and natural beauty, makes it a culturally fascinating region of India.



Tradition of Meghalaya

Meghalaya has rich and diverse traditions deeply rooted in its tribal heritage, social practices, and connection with nature. Here's an overview of some key traditional aspects of the state:

1. Matrilineal System

  • Lineage & Inheritance: Unlike most societies, Meghalaya follows a matrilineal system where lineage and inheritance pass through the female line.
  • Khadduh: The youngest daughter (Khadduh) inherits the ancestral property and is responsible for taking care of elderly parents.
  • Maternal Uncle: The mother's brother (Kni) plays an important role in family decisions and the upbringing of children.
  • Clan System: Each person belongs to their mother's clan (Kur for Khasis, Machong for Garos), and marriage within the same clan is prohibited.

2. Traditional Clothing

Khasi Attire

  • Jainsem & Dhara: Traditional attire for Khasi women, consisting of two pieces of cloth draped elegantly.
  • Jymphong: A sleeveless coat worn by Khasi men during ceremonies.
  • Kyrshah: A turban worn by Khasi men during special occasions.

Garo Attire

  • Dakmanda: Colorful woven cloth worn by Garo women.
  • Doksari: Traditional blouse worn with the Dakmanda.
  • Gando: Loincloth worn by Garo men.

Jaintia Attire

  • Thoh Khyrwang: Similar to the Khasi Jainsem, worn by Jaintia women.

3. Marriage Traditions

  • Khasi Marriage: Traditionally arranged by families, with the groom moving to the bride's house if she is the Khadduh (youngest daughter).
  • Garo Marriage: The Nokna (heiress) brings her husband (Nokrom) to live in her parents' house.
  • Clan Exogamy: Marriage within the same clan is strictly prohibited across all tribes.
  • Bride Price: In Garo tradition, the groom's family gives a symbolic bride price to the bride's family.

4. Sacred Groves & Environmental Traditions

  • Law Kyntang: Sacred forests protected by traditional religious beliefs and taboos.
  • Mawphlang Sacred Grove: One of the most famous sacred groves, believed to be protected by the local deity "U Basa."
  • Traditional Conservation: Rules against cutting trees or hunting in sacred areas have helped preserve biodiversity.
  • Living Root Bridges: A sustainable engineering tradition where Khasi and Jaintia tribes train the roots of rubber trees to form bridges over streams.

5. Traditional Governance

  • Dorbar Shnong: Traditional Khasi village council headed by a Rangbah Shnong (headman).
  • Nokma: The village headman in Garo society, who administers the village along with the council.
  • Syiemship: Traditional Khasi kingdoms headed by a Syiem (king), which still exist alongside modern governance.
  • Doloi: Traditional administrative head in Jaintia Hills.

6. Traditional Music & Dance

  • Khasi Music: Traditional instruments include Duitara (string instrument), Ksing (drums), and Tangmuri (flute).
  • Garo Music: Instruments like Nagra (drums), Chigring (string instrument), and Adil (bamboo flute).
  • Ka Shad Suk Mynsiem: Khasi dance performed during spring to thank God for good harvest.
  • Wangala Dance: Garo harvest dance performed with drums and colorful attire.
  • Behdienkhlam Dance: Jaintia dance performed during the festival to drive away plague and illness.


Meghalaya's traditions beautifully combine matrilineal social systems, environmental conservation, music, dance, and tribal governance, making it one of the most culturally unique states in India.



Festivals of Meghalaya

Meghalaya is known for its vibrant and diverse festivals, which reflect its rich tribal heritage, agricultural cycles, and religious beliefs. Here's a list of some of the major festivals celebrated in the state:

1. Shad Suk Mynsiem (Khasi Festival)

  • A thanksgiving dance festival celebrated by the Khasi tribe to express gratitude for a good harvest.
  • Men and women in traditional attire perform dances at the Weiking Ground in Shillong.
  • Men play traditional instruments while women dance in a circle wearing elaborate gold and silver ornaments.
  • The festival also celebrates the matrilineal tradition of the Khasis.

📍 Time: April


2. Behdienkhlam (Jaintia Festival)

  • One of the most important festivals of the Jaintia tribe, celebrated to drive away plague and disease.
  • Young men build tall, decorated wooden structures called "Khnongs" and "Symbuh Ksiar."
  • The festival includes rituals like beating the roofs of houses with bamboo poles to drive away evil spirits.
  • A football match played with a wooden ball is a unique feature of this festival.

📍 Time: July


3. Wangala (Garo Festival)

  • Known as the "Hundred Drums Festival," Wangala is the most important post-harvest festival of the Garo tribe.
  • Celebrated to honor Saljong, the Sun God, and to thank him for blessing the fields with a good harvest.
  • Features traditional dance performed to the rhythmic beating of drums (Dama) and blowing of horns (Nagra).
  • Men and women in colorful traditional attire dance in lines, moving forward and backward to the beat of drums.

📍 Time: November


4. Nongkrem Dance Festival (Khasi Festival)

  • A five-day religious festival celebrated by the Khasi tribe at Smit, near Shillong.
  • Performed to appease the Goddess Ka Blei Synshar for a good harvest and prosperity of the people.
  • The Syiem (chief) of Hima Khyriem performs rituals and sacrifices a goat.
  • Young unmarried women in traditional attire dance in a circle, while men perform the "Ka Shad Mastieh" dance with swords and feather crowns.

📍 Time: November


5. Ka Pomblang Nongkrem

  • An ancient religious festival of the Khasi tribe held at the Iing Sad (royal residence) in Smit.
  • Involves the sacrifice of a goat to appease the deity for good harvest and prosperity.
  • The Syiem (chief) and the Syiem Sad (priestess) perform important rituals.

📍 Time: November

6. Shad Sukmynsiem

  • A religious dance festival of the Khasi tribe celebrated at Weiking Ground in Shillong.
  • Men and women in traditional attire perform dances to thank God for the good harvest.
  • Women wear elaborate gold and silver ornaments and dance in a circle while men play traditional instruments.

📍 Time: April


7. Laho Dance

  • A harvest festival celebrated by the Pnar community of the Jaintia Hills.
  • Young men and women perform traditional dances to express gratitude for a good harvest.

📍 Time: February-March

8. Shad Suk Mynsiem

  • A thanksgiving dance festival of the Khasi tribe.
  • Celebrates the arrival of spring and expresses gratitude for a good harvest.
  • Men and women in traditional attire perform dances at the Weiking Ground in Shillong.

📍 Time: April


9. Chad Sukra

  • A religious festival celebrated by the Khasi tribe to honor their ancestors.
  • Involves rituals and offerings to seek blessings from the departed souls.

📍 Time: November-December


10. Christmas

  • With over 70% of the population being Christian, Christmas is widely celebrated across Meghalaya.
  • Shillong is beautifully decorated with lights, and midnight mass is held in churches.
  • Carol singing, feasting, and gift-giving are common practices.

📍 Time: December 25


11. Autumn Festival

  • A tourism festival organized by the Meghalaya Tourism Department to showcase the state's culture, music, and cuisine.
  • Features cultural performances, food stalls, handicraft exhibitions, and adventure sports.

📍 Time: October-November

12. Shillong Cherry Blossom Festival

  • Celebrates the blooming of cherry blossoms in Shillong, the first of its kind in India.
  • Features music concerts, fashion shows, art competitions, and food stalls.

📍 Time: November


13. Ka Pom-Blang Nongkrem

  • A five-day religious festival of the Khasi tribe held at Smit, the cultural center of the Khasis.
  • Involves rituals performed by the Syiem (chief) and the Syiem Sad (priestess) to appease the deities for good harvest and prosperity.

📍 Time: November

14. Strawberry Festival

  • Celebrates the strawberry harvest in Meghalaya, particularly in the Sohra (Cherrapunji) region.
  • Features strawberry-based products, cultural performances, and food stalls.

📍 Time: February-March



Meghalaya's festivals are a vibrant celebration of the state's tribal heritage, agricultural cycles, and religious beliefs. They offer a glimpse into the rich cultural tapestry of this beautiful northeastern state. 🎉



Foods of Meghalaya

Meghalayan cuisine is simple yet flavorful, with each tribe having its own unique culinary traditions. The food is characterized by the use of local ingredients, minimal spices, and traditional cooking methods. Here's a look at some of the most iconic foods of Meghalaya:

1. Staple Food of Meghalaya

  • Rice: The main staple food, consumed with meat, fish, or vegetable dishes.
  • Putharo: Rice pancakes made from ground rice, popular among the Khasis.
  • Pumaloi: Steamed rice cakes made from red rice, a Khasi delicacy.

2. Khasi Cuisine

  • Jadoh: A popular rice dish cooked with pork or chicken, flavored with turmeric and ginger.
  • Doh Neiiong: Pork cooked with black sesame seeds, a signature Khasi dish.
  • Tungrymbai: Fermented soybean dish, often served with rice.
  • Doh Snam: Blood sausage made with pork blood and rice.

3. Garo Cuisine

  • Nakham Bitchi: Dried fish with chili, a popular Garo dish.
  • Wak Pura: Pork cooked with bamboo shoots.
  • Do'o Makkal: Pork intestines stuffed with blood and rice.
  • Mi Mik: Rice cooked with meat and vegetables.

4. Jaintia Cuisine

  • Doh Khleh: Pork salad mixed with onions, ginger, and chili.
  • Tung-toh: Fermented soybean dish similar to Tungrymbai.
  • Jhur Sideh: Mixed vegetable stew.

5. Meat Dishes

  • Doh Khleh: Pork salad with onions, ginger, and chili.
  • Doh Sniang: Pork intestines cooked with various spices.
  • Doh Sein: Beef curry cooked with minimal spices.
  • Doh Thad: Smoked meat, usually pork or beef.

6. Fish Dishes

  • Doh Ktung: Fish curry cooked with turmeric and ginger.
  • Nakham Bitchi: Dried fish with chili, popular among the Garos.
  • Dohshain: Dried fish cooked with vegetables.

7. Vegetable Dishes

  • Jhur: Mixed vegetable stew.
  • Jali: Rice cooked with vegetables.
  • Kappa: Cucumber salad.
  • Jyrdoh: Bamboo shoot curry.
  • Nei-iong: Dishes cooked with black sesame seeds.

8. Snacks and Street Food

  • Pukhlein: Deep-fried rice cakes, a popular Khasi snack.
  • Pusla: Rice flour pancakes.
  • Jhur Sie: Vegetable fritters.
  • Kyat: Rice beer, a popular alcoholic beverage.
  • Kwai: Betel nut with betel leaf, lime, and tobacco, chewed as a digestive.

9. Beverages

  • Kyat: Rice beer, a traditional alcoholic beverage.
  • Ka Kiad: A stronger alcoholic drink made from rice or millet.
  • Sha-Shi: Black tea, often served with milk and sugar.

10. Desserts and Sweets

  • Pukhlein: Sweet deep-fried rice cakes.
  • Shana Jhur: Sweet potato pudding.
  • Til Ladoo: Sesame seed balls with jaggery.


Meghalayan cuisine is a reflection of the state's tribal culture and natural resources. The food is simple, nutritious, and deeply connected to the local environment. Whether it's the fermented soybean dishes of the Khasis, the dried fish preparations of the Garos, or the pork delicacies of the Jaintias, Meghalayan food offers a unique culinary experience. 🍛🍖🍚



History of Meghalaya

Meghalaya has a rich and diverse history that spans ancient tribal settlements, colonial rule, and the modern era. The region has played a significant role in Northeast India's cultural and political landscape.

1. Ancient Period (Before 1700s)

  • Early Settlements: The Khasi, Garo, and Jaintia tribes are believed to have migrated to the region in ancient times. The Khasis and Jaintias are of Mon-Khmer origin, while the Garos belong to the Tibeto-Burman family.
  • Tribal Kingdoms: The region was divided into small tribal kingdoms or chiefdoms. The Khasis had their Syiemships (kingdoms), the Jaintias had the Jaintia Kingdom, and the Garos had their Nokmaships (clan-based governance).
  • Jaintia Kingdom: The Jaintia Kingdom, with its capital at Jaintiapur (now in Bangladesh), was one of the most powerful kingdoms in the region.

2. Pre-Colonial Period (1700s - 1826)

  • Ahom Influence: The Ahom Kingdom of Assam had some influence over parts of the Khasi and Jaintia Hills.
  • Jaintia Kingdom: The Jaintia Kingdom, under Raja Ram Singh, reached its zenith in the 18th century.
  • Khasi Syiemships: The Khasi Hills were divided into several Syiemships (kingdoms), each ruled by a Syiem (king).
  • Garo Hills: The Garo Hills remained largely autonomous, with each village governed by a Nokma (headman).

3. British Colonial Period (1826 - 1947)

  • Treaty of Yandaboo (1826): After the First Anglo-Burmese War, the British gained control over Assam and started expanding their influence to the neighboring hill regions.
  • Annexation of Jaintia Hills (1835): The British annexed the Jaintia Hills after a dispute with the Jaintia king.
  • Anglo-Khasi War (1829-1833): The Khasis, led by U Tirot Sing, fought against British expansion. After four years of resistance, the British eventually subdued the Khasi forces.
  • Annexation of Khasi Hills: By 1833, most of the Khasi Syiemships had come under British control, though they maintained some autonomy.
  • Garo Hills Annexation (1872): The British formally annexed the Garo Hills after a series of expeditions.
  • Administrative Changes: The region was initially part of Bengal Province, then Assam Province after 1874.
  • Christian Missionaries: The arrival of Welsh missionaries in the 1840s led to the spread of Christianity and education in the Khasi and Jaintia Hills.

4. Post-Independence Period (1947 - 1972)

  • After India's independence in 1947, the Khasi, Jaintia, and Garo Hills became part of Assam state.
  • The tribal leaders of these hills began to demand a separate state to preserve their cultural identity and autonomy.
  • The All Party Hill Leaders Conference (APHLC) was formed in 1960 to advocate for a separate hill state.
  • The Assam Reorganisation (Meghalaya) Act of 1969 granted the Meghalaya Autonomous State within Assam, which was inaugurated on April 2, 1970.

5. Formation of Meghalaya State (1972)

  • On January 21, 1972, Meghalaya was established as a full-fledged state of India.
  • The state was created by carving out the Khasi, Jaintia, and Garo Hills from Assam.
  • Shillong, which had been the capital of Assam, became the capital of Meghalaya.
  • Captain Williamson Sangma became the first Chief Minister of Meghalaya.

6. Modern Meghalaya (1972 - Present)

  • Political Developments:
    • Meghalaya has seen various regional and national political parties governing the state.
    • The state has had periods of political instability with frequent changes in government.
    • The traditional institutions like the Syiemships and Dorbar Shnongs continue to exist alongside modern democratic institutions.
  • Economic Development:
    • The state's economy is primarily agrarian, with a significant portion of the population engaged in agriculture and allied activities.
    • Tourism has emerged as a major industry, with the state's natural beauty attracting visitors from across the world.
    • Mining, particularly of coal and limestone, has been an important economic activity, though it has faced environmental challenges.
  • Cultural Preservation:
    • Efforts have been made to preserve the unique cultural heritage of the Khasi, Garo, and Jaintia tribes.
    • Traditional institutions and practices continue to play a significant role in the social and cultural life of the people.

7. Notable Historical Figures

  • U Tirot Sing: A Khasi chief who led the resistance against British expansion in the Khasi Hills (1829-1833). He is revered as a freedom fighter and his death anniversary is observed as a state holiday.
  • U Kiang Nangbah: A Jaintia leader who led a revolt against the British in 1862. He was captured and hanged by the British.
  • Pa Togan Sangma: A Garo chief who fought against the British in the late 19th century.
  • Captain Williamson Sangma: The first Chief Minister of Meghalaya and a key figure in the movement for a separate hill state.

8. Recent Developments

  • Border Issues: Meghalaya has had border disputes with Assam, which have occasionally led to tensions.
  • Environmental Concerns: The state has faced challenges related to deforestation, mining, and climate change.
  • Tourism Growth: Meghalaya has seen a significant increase in tourism, with places like Cherrapunji, Mawlynnong, and the living root bridges becoming popular attractions.
  • Cultural Renaissance: There has been a renewed interest in preserving and promoting the traditional culture, music, and arts of the state.

9. Present-Day Meghalaya

  • Cultural Hub: Shillong is known as the "Rock Capital of India" with a vibrant music scene.
  • Economic Growth: Tourism, agriculture, and service sectors are key economic drivers.
  • Tourist Attractions: Living Root Bridges, Cherrapunji, Mawsynram, Dawki River, and Shillong are major tourist destinations.


Meghalaya's history is a fascinating blend of tribal traditions, colonial encounters, and modern developments. From the ancient tribal kingdoms to the formation of a modern state, Meghalaya has maintained its unique cultural identity while adapting to changing times. 🚩📜



Art & Craft of Meghalaya 🎨🖌️

Meghalaya has a rich artistic heritage, with each tribe having its own unique crafts and art forms. The art and craft of Meghalaya reflect the close relationship between the people and nature, with materials like bamboo, cane, wood, and natural fibers being commonly used.

1. Bamboo & Cane Craft 🏺

  • Baskets: Various types of baskets for different purposes, from carrying loads to storing grains.
  • Khoh: A traditional Khasi bamboo basket used for carrying goods.
  • Memangtong: A Garo bamboo basket used for storing grains.
  • Furniture: Chairs, tables, and other furniture items made from bamboo and cane.
  • Mats: Intricately woven bamboo mats used for sitting and sleeping.

2. Textile & Weaving 🧵

🔸 Khasi Textiles

  • Jainsem: Traditional dress worn by Khasi women, with intricate designs and patterns.
  • Jymphong: Traditional attire for Khasi men, often adorned with tribal motifs.
  • Kyrshah: A turban worn by Khasi men during ceremonies.

🔸 Garo Textiles

  • Dakmanda: Colorful woven cloth worn by Garo women.
  • Doksari: Traditional blouse worn with the Dakmanda.
  • Gando: Loincloth worn by Garo men.

🔸 Jaintia Textiles

  • Thoh Khyrwang: Similar to the Khasi Jainsem, worn by Jaintia women.

3. Wood Carving 🌳

  • Ceremonial Objects: Wooden masks, statues, and ceremonial objects used in traditional rituals.
  • Household Items: Wooden utensils, bowls, and containers.
  • Decorative Items: Intricately carved wooden panels and decorative pieces.

4. Jewelry Making 💍

  • Traditional Jewelry: Handcrafted jewelry made from gold, silver, and brass.
  • Kynjri Ksiar: Gold necklace worn by Khasi women during ceremonies.
  • Rigitok: Silver earrings worn by Garo women.
  • Sohshkor: Coral beads worn by Khasi women.

5. Musical Instruments 🎵

  • Duitara: A stringed instrument used by the Khasis, made from wood and gourd.
  • Ksing: Traditional Khasi drums made from wood and animal hide.
  • Chigring: A Garo stringed instrument similar to a guitar.
  • Nagra: Garo drums used during festivals and ceremonies.
  • Tangmuri: A wind instrument made from bamboo.

6. Pottery 🏺

  • Traditional Pottery: Handcrafted pottery items for household use.
  • Ceremonial Vessels: Special pottery items used during religious ceremonies.

7. Blacksmithing ⚒️

  • Agricultural Tools: Handcrafted tools for farming and agriculture.
  • Weapons: Traditional weapons like knives and spears.
  • Household Items: Iron utensils and tools for daily use.

8. Living Root Bridges 🌉

  • While not a traditional craft in the conventional sense, the living root bridges of Meghalaya are a unique form of bio-engineering.
  • The Khasi and Jaintia tribes train the roots of rubber trees (Ficus elastica) to grow across streams and rivers, creating natural bridges.
  • These bridges can last for hundreds of years and grow stronger over time.
  • Famous examples include the Double Decker Root Bridge in Nongriat and the Long Root Bridge in Mawlynnong.

Meghalaya, known as the "Abode of Clouds," experiences a unique climate due to its geographical location and elevation. The state receives some of the highest rainfall in the world, particularly in Cherrapunji and Mawsynram. Here's a guide to the best seasons to visit this beautiful northeastern state.



Season-wise Breakdown for Visiting Meghalaya

🌸 Spring (March - April)

  • Pleasant weather with temperatures ranging from 15°C to 25°C.
  • Ideal for visiting Shillong, Cherrapunji, and the living root bridges.
  • The landscape is lush and green, with flowers in bloom.
  • Perfect time for outdoor activities like trekking and camping.

☀️ Summer (May – June)

  • Early summer (May) is still pleasant with temperatures between 20°C and 30°C.
  • By June, the monsoon begins to set in, with occasional showers.
  • Good time to visit Shillong, Dawki River, and Mawlynnong (Asia's cleanest village).
  • The waterfalls begin to come alive as the rains start.

🌧️ Monsoon (July - September)

  • The wettest period in Meghalaya, with heavy rainfall, especially in Cherrapunji and Mawsynram.
  • Waterfalls are at their majestic best, including Nohkalikai Falls, Elephant Falls, and Seven Sisters Falls.
  • Travel can be challenging due to landslides and poor road conditions.
  • Not recommended for trekking or outdoor activities.
  • The living root bridges are slippery and can be dangerous to visit during heavy rains.

🍁 Autumn (October - November)

  • One of the best seasons to visit Meghalaya, with clear skies and pleasant weather.
  • Temperatures range from 12°C to 25°C.
  • Perfect time for trekking, camping, and exploring the living root bridges.
  • The landscape is lush green after the monsoon, and the waterfalls are still flowing.
  • The Autumn Festival and Cherry Blossom Festival are held during this time.

❄️ Winter (December - February)

  • Cold but pleasant, with temperatures ranging from 5°C to 20°C.
  • Clear skies offer stunning views of the landscape.
  • Perfect time for sightseeing and photography.
  • Some higher elevations might experience frost.
  • Christmas is widely celebrated across the state, especially in Shillong.
  • The Wangala Festival (Garo harvest festival) is celebrated in November-December.

Tourist Spots of Meghalaya 🏞️🌉

Meghalaya, the "Abode of Clouds," is a paradise for nature lovers, adventure enthusiasts, and those seeking unique cultural experiences. Here's a categorized list of the best tourist places in Meghalaya:



1. Waterfalls & Natural Wonders 🌊

  • Nohkalikai Falls - India's tallest plunge waterfall, dropping from a height of 1,115 feet near Cherrapunji.
  • Seven Sisters Falls (Nohsngithiang) - Seven streams falling side by side, creating a spectacular sight during the monsoon.
  • Elephant Falls - A three-tier waterfall near Shillong, named for a rock resembling an elephant (which no longer exists due to an earthquake).
  • Krang Suri Falls - A hidden gem in the Jaintia Hills with turquoise blue waters.
  • Wei Sawdong Falls - A three-tier waterfall with crystal clear blue water and a viewing platform.
  • Dainthlen Falls - Associated with a legend about the killing of a giant serpent.

2. Living Root Bridges 🌉

  • Double Decker Root Bridge (Nongriat) - A unique two-tier bridge made from the living roots of rubber trees.
  • Single Root Bridge (Mawlynnong) - An easier-to-access living root bridge in Asia's cleanest village.
  • Long Root Bridge (Pynursla) - One of the longest living root bridges in Meghalaya.
  • Ritymmen Root Bridge - Considered one of the strongest root bridges, capable of holding 50 people at once.

3. Caves & Rock Formations 🏞️

  • Mawsmai Cave - A limestone cave near Cherrapunji with impressive stalactite and stalagmite formations.
  • Arwah Cave - Known for its fossils and wide chambers.
  • Siju Cave - One of the longest caves in the Indian subcontinent, home to thousands of bats.
  • Krem Liat Prah - The longest cave in India, part of the Shnongrim Ridge cave system.

4. Lakes & Rivers 💦

  • Umiam Lake - A man-made reservoir near Shillong, offering boating and water sports.
  • Dawki River - Known for its crystal clear waters, where boats appear to be floating in air.
  • Ward's Lake - A picturesque lake in the heart of Shillong with boating facilities.
  • Thadlaskein Lake - A man-made lake in the Jaintia Hills with a fascinating legend.
  • Umngot River - One of the cleanest rivers in Asia, flowing through Dawki.

5. Sacred Groves & Natural Reserves 🌳

  • Mawphlang Sacred Grove - A preserved forest with ancient trees and rich biodiversity.
  • Nokrek Biosphere Reserve - A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, home to the rare Nokrek citrus fruit.
  • Balpakram National Park - Known as the "land of perpetual winds" with diverse flora and fauna.
  • Law Lyngdoh Sacred Grove - A sacred forest in Mawsynram with ancient trees and medicinal plants.

6. Cultural & Heritage Sites 🏛️

  • Mawlynnong Village - Known as "Asia's Cleanest Village" with its bamboo sky walks and living root bridges.
  • Don Bosco Museum - A seven-story museum in Shillong showcasing the culture of Northeast India.
  • Nartiang Monoliths - One of the largest collections of monoliths in one place.
  • Shillong Peak - The highest point in Shillong, offering panoramic views of the city.
  • Laitlum Canyons - Offering breathtaking views of deep gorges and valleys.

7. Adventure & Trekking Spots 🥾

  • David Scott Trail - A 16 km trek following a path built by a British officer in the 1800s.
  • Nongriat Trek - A challenging trek to the Double Decker Root Bridge with natural swimming pools.
  • Khasi Hills - Offering numerous trekking routes through forests and villages.
  • Kudengrim - A paradise for caving enthusiasts with numerous unexplored caves.
  • Mawryngkhang Trek (Sky Walk) - A bamboo walkway built along a ridge, offering thrilling views.


Wildlife & Crops of Meghalaya 🌿

Meghalaya, with its diverse ecosystems ranging from tropical forests to alpine meadows, is home to a rich variety of flora and fauna. The state's agricultural landscape is equally diverse, with traditional farming practices adapted to the hilly terrain.

Meghalaya's forests, sacred groves, and protected areas harbor a wide range of wildlife species, many of which are endemic to the region.

🌾 Agriculture & Major Crops in Meghalaya

Agriculture is the main occupation in Meghalaya, with about 80% of the population dependent on it for their livelihood. Traditional farming practices like jhum (slash and burn) cultivation are still practiced in some areas.

🔹 Key Agricultural Features

  • Terrace Farming: Practiced on hillsides to prevent soil erosion.
  • Jhum Cultivation: Traditional slash and burn agriculture, though being replaced by more sustainable methods.
  • Organic Farming: Many farmers practice organic farming, especially in areas like Mawlynnong.
  • Climate: High rainfall and moderate temperatures support a variety of crops.

🔹 Special Agricultural Products

  • Lakadong Turmeric: Known for its high curcumin content, grown in the Jaintia Hills.
  • Khasi Mandarin Orange: Sweet and juicy oranges grown in the Khasi Hills.
  • Meghalaya Strawberry: Grown in Sohra (Cherrapunji) region, known for its sweetness.
  • Nepenthes khasiana: The only pitcher plant species in India, endemic to Meghalaya.

Sustainable Agriculture & Modern Trends 🌾

  • Organic Farming: Many farmers in Meghalaya practice organic farming, especially in areas like Mawlynnong.
  • Integrated Farming: Combining agriculture with fishery and livestock.
  • Apiculture: Honey production is an important supplementary income source.
  • Floriculture: Growing of ornamental flowers, especially orchids, is gaining popularity.


Meghalaya's rich biodiversity and agricultural practices are deeply intertwined with the state's cultural heritage. The traditional knowledge of the Khasi, Garo, and Jaintia tribes regarding the use of plants for food, medicine, and other purposes is invaluable and is being preserved through various conservation efforts.



🎓 Education & Institutions in Meghalaya

Meghalaya has made significant strides in education since its formation as a state in 1972. The state has a mix of government and private educational institutions, with a focus on improving literacy rates and providing quality education to its tribal population.



📌 Education System in Meghalaya

  • School Education: Follows the Meghalaya Board of School Education (MBOSE) for secondary and higher secondary education. CBSE and ICSE schools are also present.
  • Higher Education: Managed by universities and colleges under UGC and state government.
  • Literacy Rate: 74.4% as per the 2011 census, with efforts to improve it further.
  • Medium of Instruction: English is the primary medium of instruction in most schools and colleges.

🏛️ Top Universities & Institutions

🔹 Universities

  3. Martin Luther Christian University - A private university focusing on health sciences, social work, and rural development.
  4. CMJ University - A private university offering various undergraduate and postgraduate programs.
  5. William Carey University - A private university with a focus on theology, education, and social sciences.

🔹 Colleges

  2. Lady Keane College - A women's college in Shillong, established in 1935.
  3. Shillong College - Established in 1956, offering various undergraduate and postgraduate programs.

Meghalaya has several research institutions focusing on various fields:

📚 Challenges & Initiatives

  1. Geographical Challenges: The hilly terrain and scattered settlements make it difficult to establish and access educational institutions in remote areas.
  2. Infrastructure Development: The government is working on improving educational infrastructure, especially in rural areas.
  3. Digital Education: Efforts are being made to bridge the digital divide and promote online education.
  4. Tribal Education: Special initiatives are being implemented to promote education among the tribal population, including scholarships and reservation policies.
  5. Language Preservation: Efforts are being made to preserve and promote the indigenous languages of Meghalaya through education.
