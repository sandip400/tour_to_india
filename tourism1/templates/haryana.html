Culture of Haryana

Haryana has a rich and vibrant culture deeply rooted in its agricultural heritage, martial traditions, and folk customs. The state's culture is characterized by its rustic simplicity, warmth, and a strong sense of community. Here's an overview of some key aspects of Haryana's culture:

1. Language

  • Haryanvi, a dialect of Hindi, is the primary language spoken in Haryana. It has its own distinct vocabulary, pronunciation, and expressions that reflect the rural ethos of the region. Hindi is the official language, while Punjabi is also spoken in some parts, especially near the Punjab border.

2. Literature and Oral Traditions

  • Haryana has a rich tradition of folk tales, ballads, and proverbs that have been passed down through generations. The state has produced notable writers like Surendra Sharma and Vishnu Prabhakar.
  • Ragini, a form of folk song that narrates stories of valor, love, and social issues, is an important part of Haryanvi literature.
  • Swang, a traditional form of folk theater, combines dialogue, music, dance, and comedy to convey social messages.


3. Art and Craft

  • Phulkari embroidery, featuring colorful floral designs, is a traditional craft practiced by women in Haryana.
  • Pottery, especially the black pottery of Jhajjar, is known for its unique designs and techniques.
  • Durrie (cotton rugs) weaving is another traditional craft, with geometric patterns and bright colors.
  • Wooden furniture with intricate carvings, especially from Karnal and Faridabad, is famous throughout North India.

4. Music and Dance

  • Haryanvi folk music is characterized by its simplicity and rustic charm, often accompanied by traditional instruments like dhol, nagara, and sarangi.
  • Ragini, a form of ballad singing, is popular during social gatherings and festivals.
  • Folk dances include:
    • Ghoomar: Performed by women during festive occasions, involving graceful circular movements.
    • Jhumar: A male folk dance depicting the joy of harvest season.
    • Dhamal: An energetic dance performed by men during Holi and other celebrations.
    • Khoria: A dance performed by elderly women during weddings.
    • Loor: A romantic dance performed by young girls during the monsoon season.

5. Cuisine

  • Haryanvi cuisine is simple, nutritious, and hearty, reflecting the agricultural lifestyle of the region.
  • Staple foods include bajra and wheat rotis, often served with ghee, lassi, and buttermilk.
  • Popular dishes include:
    • Kadhi Pakora: Gram flour dumplings in a yogurt-based curry.
    • Bajra Khichdi: A nutritious mix of millet and lentils.
    • Bathua Raita: A yogurt dish made with bathua leaves.
    • Methi Gajar: A vegetable dish made with fenugreek leaves and carrots.
    • Kachri Ki Sabzi: A traditional dish made from wild melons.
    • Malpua and Kheer: Popular sweet dishes.

6. Attire

  • Traditional Haryanvi men wear dhoti-kurta with a turban (pagri) and a waistcoat (sadri).
  • Women traditionally wear ghagra-choli (long skirt and blouse) with a head covering (odhni).
  • Heavy silver jewelry, including anklets, bangles, and necklaces, is an important part of women's attire.


7. Sports and Martial Traditions

  • Haryana has a strong sporting culture, particularly in wrestling (kushti), boxing, and kabaddi.
  • The state has produced numerous Olympic medalists and international champions, especially in wrestling and boxing.
  • Traditional akharas (wrestling pits) are common in villages, where young men train in wrestling.
  • Gatka, a traditional martial art, is practiced in some parts of the state.

8. Religious Practices

  • Hinduism is the predominant religion, with significant worship of deities like Lord Shiva, Krishna, and local folk deities.
  • Kurukshetra, the battlefield of the Mahabharata, holds immense religious significance.
  • Folk deities like Gugga Pir and Baba Khatu Shyam are widely revered.
  • Sikhism has a significant presence, especially in districts bordering Punjab.

9. Festivals

  • Teej: Celebrated by women during Festivals
    • Teej: Celebrated by women during the monsoon season with swings, songs, and prayers for marital bliss.
    • Lohri: A winter harvest festival celebrated with bonfires, folk songs, and traditional dances.
    • Baisakhi: Marks the beginning of the harvest season, celebrated with folk dances and community gatherings.
    • Gugga Naumi: A festival dedicated to Gugga Pir, believed to protect against snakebites and other dangers.
    • Holi: Celebrated with great enthusiasm, featuring the unique Haryanvi tradition of "Dulandi" (celebrated a day after Holi).

10. Social Structure and Values

  • Haryana has a traditional patriarchal society with strong family bonds and respect for elders.
  • The khap panchayat (clan council) still plays a significant role in rural social governance, though their influence is gradually changing.
  • Hospitality is a core value, with guests treated with great respect and warmth.
  • The concept of "Dhaakad" (bravery) is highly valued, especially for men.

11. Rural Traditions

  • Chaupal: A community space in villages where people gather to discuss issues, settle disputes, and socialize.
  • Hookah: The traditional water pipe is often shared among men during social gatherings.
  • Gotras: Clan-based social organization remains important, especially in marriage alliances.
  • Agricultural festivals and rituals mark different stages of the farming cycle.

12. Modern Cultural Developments

  • Haryanvi music and films have gained popularity beyond the state, with artists like Daler Mehndi and Sapna Chaudhary achieving national fame.
  • The state has seen a rise in sports academies and training centers, further strengthening its sporting culture.
  • Urban centers like Gurugram have developed a cosmopolitan culture while still maintaining connections to traditional Haryanvi roots.

Tradition of Haryana

Haryana has a rich tapestry of traditions deeply rooted in its agricultural heritage, rural lifestyle, and historical influences. These traditions reflect the state's values of community, hospitality, and resilience. Here's an overview of some key traditional aspects of the state:

1. Clothing Tradition

Men

  • Dhoti-Kurta: Traditional attire consisting of a long shirt and loose-fitting lower garment.
  • Pagri (Turban): An essential part of traditional dress, symbolizing honor and dignity.
  • Sadri: A sleeveless jacket worn over the kurta, especially during cooler months.
  • Lungi: A shorter version of dhoti, often worn during agricultural work.

Women

  • Ghagra-Choli: A long skirt worn with a blouse, often in bright colors with embroidery.
  • Odhni/Dupatta: A head covering that is an essential part of traditional attire.
  • Kurti-Salwar: A more contemporary adaptation, popular in both rural and urban areas.
  • Jewelry: Silver anklets (payal), toe rings (bichhiya), bangles (chuda), and necklaces (hansli) are traditional ornaments.

2. Wedding Traditions

  • Sagai (Engagement): Involves the exchange of rings and gifts between families.
  • Baan: A pre-wedding ritual where the groom's family sends gifts to the bride's family.
  • Mehndi: Intricate henna designs are applied to the bride's hands and feet.
  • Ghurchari: The groom's ceremonial procession on horseback to the bride's home.
  • Doli: The emotional departure of the bride from her parental home in a decorated palanquin.
  • Gotras: Marriage within the same gotra (clan) is traditionally prohibited.

3. Music & Dance Traditions

  • Ragini: A form of folk song that narrates stories of valor, love, and social issues.
  • Saang/Swang: Traditional folk theater combining dialogue, music, dance, and comedy.
  • Ghoomar: A graceful dance performed by women in circular formations.
  • Jhumar: A male folk dance celebrating the harvest season.
  • Dhamal: An energetic dance performed during festivals, especially Holi.
  • Phag: Songs and dances associated with the spring season and Holi festival.

4. Social & Community Traditions

  • Chaupal: A community space in villages where people gather to discuss issues, settle disputes, and socialize.
  • Khap Panchayat: Traditional clan councils that govern social norms and resolve disputes.
  • Hookah: The traditional water pipe shared among men during social gatherings, symbolizing community bonding.
  • Teej Celebration: Women gather to sing, dance, and pray for marital bliss during the monsoon season.
  • Gotras: Clan-based social organization that determines marriage alliances and community relations.

5. Agricultural Traditions

  • Baisakhi: Harvest festival marking the beginning of the agricultural year.

  • Seasonal Rituals: Various ceremonies mark different stages of the agricultural cycle, from sowing to harvesting.
  • Cattle Fairs: Traditional gatherings for trading livestock, especially bullocks and cows.
  • Guga Naumi: Festival dedicated to Guga Pir, believed to protect crops and cattle from snakes and natural calamities.

6. Culinary Traditions

  • Kachri Ki Sabzi: A traditional dish made from wild melons.

Festivals of Haryana

Haryana celebrates a variety of festivals that reflect its agricultural heritage, religious beliefs, and cultural traditions. These festivals bring communities together and showcase the vibrant spirit of Haryanvi culture. Here's a list of some of the major festivals celebrated in the state:

1. Teej

  • A monsoon festival celebrated primarily by women to welcome the rainy season.
  • Women dress in colorful clothes, apply mehndi (henna), and gather to sing and dance.
  • Swings are hung from trees, and women enjoy swinging while singing traditional songs.
  • Married women pray for their husbands' well-being, while unmarried girls pray for good husbands.
  • Special foods like ghevar (a sweet dish) and traditional savories are prepared.

2. Lohri

  • A winter harvest festival celebrated with great enthusiasm across Haryana.
  • People gather around a bonfire, throw sesame seeds, jaggery, and popcorn into it, and sing folk songs.
  • The festival marks the end of winter and the onset of longer days.
  • Special significance is given to the first Lohri of a newlywed couple or a newborn child.
  • Traditional foods include revri, gajak, peanuts, and popcorn.

  • Fairs and cultural programs are organized across the state.

4. Gugga Naumi

Foods of Haryana

Haryanvi cuisine is characterized by its simplicity, nutritional value, and rustic flavors that reflect the state's agricultural heritage. The food is hearty and wholesome, designed to provide energy for the physically demanding rural lifestyle. Here's a look at some of the most iconic foods of Haryana:

1. Staple Food of Haryana

  • Bajra Roti: Flatbread made from pearl millet flour, a staple especially during winter months.
  • Besan Masala Roti: Flatbread made from gram flour mixed with spices.
  • Wheat Roti: Simple unleavened bread made from wheat flour, a daily staple.
  • Jowar Roti: Flatbread made from sorghum flour, common in rural areas.

2. Traditional Haryanvi Meals

  • Components: A typical Haryanvi meal includes roti, sabzi (vegetable dish), dahi (yogurt), lassi (buttermilk drink), and sometimes a sweet dish.
  • Serving Style: Traditionally served on pattal (leaf plates) in rural areas, with an emphasis on fresh, home-cooked food.
  • Ghee: Liberal use of ghee (clarified butter) is a hallmark of Haryanvi cuisine, adding richness and flavor.

3. Famous Haryanvi Dishes

Vegetarian Specialties

  • Kadhi Pakora: Gram flour dumplings in a yogurt-based curry, a popular dish especially during festivals.
  • Kachri Ki Sabzi: A traditional dish made from wild melons, unique to the region.
  • Methi Gajar: A nutritious vegetable dish made with fenugreek leaves and carrots.
  • Hara Dhania Cholia: Fresh green chickpeas cooked with coriander and spices.
  • Bajra Khichdi: A nutritious one-pot meal made with pearl millet and lentils.
  • Bathua Raita: A yogurt dish made with bathua (pigweed) leaves, rich in iron and other nutrients.

Non-Vegetarian Specialties

  • Kukad Khad: A rustic chicken curry cooked on slow fire, often prepared during special occasions.
  • Masala Meat: Spicy mutton curry cooked with whole spices and served with roti.
  • Khad Murgh: Chicken marinated with spices and cooked in an earthen pot buried in the ground.
  • Seekh Kebab: Minced meat mixed with spices and grilled on skewers.
  • Junglee Maas: A spicy game meat preparation, traditionally made with wild boar or deer.

4. Haryanvi Sweets & Desserts

  • Churma: A sweet dish made by crushing wheat rotis and mixing them with ghee and sugar.
  • Meethe Chawal: Sweet rice prepared with saffron, milk, and dry fruits.
  • Kheer: Rice pudding cooked with milk, sugar, and flavored with cardamom and saffron.
  • Malpua: Sweet pancakes dipped in sugar syrup, often served during festivals.
  • Ghevar: A disc-shaped sweet cake made with flour and soaked in sugar syrup, popular during Teej festival.
  • Alsi Ki Pinni: Nutritious sweet balls made with flaxseeds, jaggery, and ghee.

5. Beverages of Haryana

  • Lassi: A yogurt-based drink, often flavored with sugar or salt, extremely popular especially during summer.
  • Chaach/Mattha: Buttermilk, a refreshing drink served with meals, sometimes flavored with cumin and mint.
  • Kanji: A fermented drink made from black carrots, mustard seeds, and water, known for its digestive properties.
  • Sattu Sharbat: A cooling drink made from roasted gram flour, popular among farmers during fieldwork.
  • Thandai: A cooling milk-based drink flavored with nuts, seeds, and spices, popular during festivals like Holi.

6. Seasonal Specialties

  • Winter Specialties:
    • Sarson Ka Saag: Mustard greens cooked with spices, traditionally served with makki di roti (cornmeal flatbread).
    • Gajar Ka Halwa: Sweet dish made from carrots, milk, and sugar, garnished with nuts.
    • Bajra Khichdi: Pearl millet cooked with lentils, especially popular during cold months.
  • Summer Specialties:
    • Raabri: A cooling porridge made from bajra flour and buttermilk.
    • Sattu Paratha: Flatbread stuffed with roasted gram flour mixture, providing energy in hot weather.
    • Kair Sangri: A dish made from desert beans and berries, preserved for use during summer.

7. Festival Foods

  • Lohri: Revri (sesame brittle), gajak (sesame and jaggery sweet), peanuts, and popcorn are traditional foods.
  • Teej: Ghevar (disc-shaped sweet cake) and other sweet dishes are prepared.
  • Holi: Gujiya (sweet dumplings), mathri (savory crackers), and thandai (spiced milk drink) are popular.
  • Baisakhi: Kadhi chawal, meethe chawal, and various sweet dishes mark the harvest festival.
  • Gugga Naumi: Churma (crushed wheat sweet) is offered to Gugga Pir and distributed as prasad.

History of Haryana

Haryana has a rich and diverse history that spans ancient civilizations, medieval kingdoms, colonial rule, and the modern era. The region has played a significant role in India's political, cultural, and economic history.

1. Ancient Haryana (Before 6th Century CE)

  • Indus Valley Civilization (3300-1300 BCE): Archaeological evidence from sites like Rakhigarhi, Banawali, and Mitathal indicates that Haryana was part of the Indus Valley Civilization, one of the world's oldest urban cultures.
  • Vedic Period (1500-500 BCE): The region was part of the Kuru Kingdom mentioned in the Vedas and Mahabharata. Kurukshetra, where the epic battle of Mahabharata is believed to have taken place, is located in present-day Haryana.
  • Maurya and Gupta Empires: The region was part of these major ancient Indian empires, with evidence of Buddhist and Hindu influences.

2. Medieval Haryana (6th - 18th Century)

Early Medieval Period (6th-12th Century):

  • The region was ruled by various dynasties including the Pushyabhutis of Thanesar, with King Harsha being a notable ruler who united much of North India in the 7th century.
  • Tomara Rajputs established their kingdom with Dhillika (Delhi) as their capital, controlling parts of present-day Haryana.

Delhi Sultanate Period (1206-1526):

  • Haryana became part of the Delhi Sultanate, with significant influence from Turkic, Afghan, and Persian cultures.
  • The region witnessed several important battles, including the three Battles of Panipat that shaped Indian history.

Mughal Period (1526-1707):

  • The First Battle of Panipat in 1526 established Mughal rule in India when Babur defeated Ibrahim Lodi.
  • The region prospered under Mughal rule, with development of infrastructure, agriculture, and culture.
  • Several important monuments and gardens were built during this period, including the Pinjore Gardens.

Post-Mughal Period (1707-1803):

  • After the decline of the Mughal Empire, the region was contested between various powers including the Marathas, Sikhs, and local chieftains.
  • The Third Battle of Panipat in 1761 between the Marathas and Ahmad Shah Abdali's forces was a pivotal moment in Indian history.
  • Jat rulers established significant control in parts of Haryana, with Raja Suraj Mal being a prominent leader.

3. Colonial Period (1803-1947)

  • British East India Company (1803-1857): The region came under British control after the Second Anglo-Maratha War, initially as part of the North-Western Provinces.
  • Revolt of 1857: Several areas in Haryana, including Rohtak, Rewari, and Jhajjar, were active centers of the rebellion against British rule.
  • British Raj (1858-1947): After the revolt, the region was reorganized as part of the Punjab Province. The British developed canal systems that transformed agriculture in the region.
  • Freedom Movement: Haryana actively participated in India's freedom struggle, with leaders like Sir Chhotu Ram advocating for farmers' rights and social reforms.

4. Post-Independence Period (1947-Present)

Partition and Its Aftermath

  • The Partition of India in 1947 significantly affected the region, with large-scale population exchanges and refugee settlements.
  • The area remained part of Punjab state after independence.

Formation of Haryana State

  • On November 1, 1966, Haryana was carved out of Punjab on linguistic grounds, becoming India's 17th state.
  • Chandigarh became a union territory and the shared capital of both Punjab and Haryana.
  • Bhagwat Dayal Sharma became the first Chief Minister of the newly formed state.

Modern Developments

  • Green Revolution: Haryana was at the forefront of India's Green Revolution in the 1960s and 1970s, transforming from a food-deficit state to a major agricultural producer.
  • Industrialization: Since the 1980s, the state has seen significant industrial growth, particularly in the automotive, IT, and manufacturing sectors.
  • Economic Hub: Gurugram (formerly Gurgaon) has emerged as a major corporate and IT hub, housing offices of numerous multinational companies.
  • Sports Excellence: Haryana has produced numerous Olympic medalists and international champions, particularly in wrestling, boxing, and shooting.

5. Cultural and Social History

Agricultural Heritage

  • Haryana has a rich agricultural tradition dating back thousands of years, with farming being central to its economy and culture.
  • The development of canal systems, first under the Mughals and later expanded by the British, transformed agriculture in the region.
  • The Green Revolution further modernized farming practices, making Haryana one of India's agricultural powerhouses.

Martial Tradition

Art & Craft of Haryana üé®üñåÔ∏è

Haryana has a rich tradition of arts and crafts that reflect the state's cultural heritage, rural lifestyle, and artistic sensibilities. These crafts have been passed down through generations, preserving traditional techniques while adapting to contemporary tastes. Here's an overview of the diverse arts and crafts of Haryana:

1. Textile Arts üßµ

üî∏ Phulkari Embroidery

  • Origin: Traditional embroidery practiced by women, especially in areas bordering Punjab.
  • Technique: Colorful floral patterns created using darn stitch on handspun cotton fabric (khaddar).
  • Products: Dupattas, shawls, bed covers, and wall hangings.
  • Significance: Traditionally created by mothers for their daughters' wedding trousseaus.

üî∏ Shisha (Mirror Work) Embroidery

  • Technique: Small mirrors are incorporated into embroidery using colorful threads.
  • Products: Clothing, cushion covers, wall hangings, and decorative items.
  • Regions: Particularly popular in southern Haryana.

2. Weaving Traditions üß∂

üî∏ Durrie (Cotton Rugs)

  • Technique: Flat-woven cotton rugs with geometric patterns and bright colors.
  • Centers: Panipat is famous as the "City of Weavers" and a major center for durrie production.
  • Features: Durable, washable, and versatile, used as floor coverings, wall hangings, and bedding.

üî∏ Khes

  • Description: Traditional cotton bedspread with checkered patterns.
  • Technique: Woven on pit looms using cotton yarn.
  • Significance: Often included in a bride's trousseau.

üî∏ Panja Durrie

  • Technique: Hand-woven using a special tool called "panja" to create tight weaves.
  • Features: Known for durability and intricate designs.

3. Pottery and Ceramics üè∫

  • Black Pottery of Jhajjar: Distinctive black pottery made using a special firing technique.
  • Terracotta Crafts: Clay figurines, toys, and decorative items made by rural artisans.
  • Technique: Traditional potter's wheel (chak) is used, with designs often painted or etched by hand.
  • Products: Water pots (matkas), storage jars, decorative items, and religious artifacts.

4. Woodwork and Furniture ü™µ

  • Carved Furniture: Intricate wood carving on furniture, especially from Karnal and Faridabad.
  • Pida (Low Stool): Traditional wooden stools with woven seats, often used in rural households.
  • Charpai (Traditional Cot): Wooden frame with woven rope or jute webbing.
  • Decorative Items: Carved wooden boxes, photo frames, and wall panels.
  • Woods Used: Primarily sheesham (Indian rosewood), mango, and neem.

5. Metal Crafts üî®

  • Brass Work: Utensils, decorative items, and religious artifacts made from brass.
  • Iron Craft: Agricultural tools, decorative items, and household utensils.
  • Bell Metal: Traditional utensils and decorative items made from an alloy of copper and tin.
  • Centers: Rewari is known for its brass and copper work.

6. Leather Craft üß≥

  • Products: Traditional footwear (juti), bags, belts, and horse accessories.
  • Technique: Hand-stitched and often embellished with embroidery or beadwork.
  • Centers: Gurgaon and Faridabad are known for leather crafts.

7. Paper Crafts üìú

  • Handmade Paper: Traditional paper making using natural fibers and eco-friendly techniques.
  • Products: Stationery, greeting cards, photo albums, and decorative items.
  • Centers: Kurukshetra and Rohtak have workshops specializing in handmade paper crafts.
  • Features: Often incorporates dried flowers, leaves, and natural dyes.

8. Bamboo and Cane Craft üéã

  • Products: Baskets, furniture, storage containers, and decorative items.
  • Technique: Weaving and binding bamboo and cane strips into functional and decorative items.
  • Utility: Used for agricultural storage, household items, and increasingly for contemporary home decor.

9. Folk Paintings üñºÔ∏è

  • Wall Paintings: Traditional murals depicting religious themes, folklore, and daily life scenes.
  • Mandana: Floor and wall paintings made during festivals and special occasions.
  • Materials: Natural pigments, clay, and cow dung are traditionally used.
  • Themes: Often depict rural life, mythology, and auspicious symbols.

10. Musical Instruments üéµ

  • Handcrafted Instruments: Traditional musical instruments like dhol, nagara, sarangi, and chimta.
  • Materials: Wood, leather, metal, and natural fibers are used.
  • Craftsmanship: Requires specialized skills passed down through generations.

Seasons to visit Haryana üåßÔ∏è‚òÄÔ∏è

Haryana experiences a continental climate with hot summers, cold winters, and a moderate monsoon season. The best time to visit depends on your interests, the regions you plan to explore, and your tolerance for different weather conditions.



Season-wise Breakdown for Visiting Haryana

‚ùÑÔ∏è Winter (October - March)

  • The most pleasant season to visit Haryana with cool, dry weather.
  • October and November offer mild temperatures (15-30¬∞C/59-86¬∞F) with clear skies.
  • December to February can be quite cold (5-20¬∞C/41-68¬∞F), with occasional fog and mist, especially in the morning.
  • Perfect time for sightseeing, outdoor activities, and exploring historical sites.
  • Major festivals like Lohri (January) and the Surajkund Crafts Mela (February) take place during this season.

üå∏ Spring (March - April)

  • A transitional season with gradually rising temperatures (15-35¬∞C/59-95¬∞F).
  • Pleasant mornings and evenings, though afternoons can get warm.
  • The countryside is often colorful with flowering trees and crops.
  • Holi (March) is celebrated with great enthusiasm across the state.
  • Good time for visiting wildlife sanctuaries and rural tourism.

‚òÄÔ∏è Summer (May - June)

  • Extremely hot and dry with temperatures often exceeding 40¬∞C (104¬∞F).
  • Heat waves are common, with temperatures sometimes reaching 45-48¬∞C (113-118¬∞F).
  • Not recommended for general tourism, especially outdoor activities.
  • If visiting during this time, early morning or evening activities are advisable.
  • Indoor attractions like museums, shopping malls, and water parks can be considered.

üåßÔ∏è Monsoon (July - September)

  • Moderate rainfall brings relief from the summer heat, with temperatures ranging from 25-35¬∞C (77-95¬∞F).
  • The landscape turns lush green, especially in rural areas and Morni Hills.
  • Humidity levels can be high, making it feel warmer than the actual temperature.
  • Occasional heavy downpours might cause waterlogging in urban areas.
  • Teej festival (July-August) is celebrated during this season.
  • Good time for photography and experiencing rural Haryana.
